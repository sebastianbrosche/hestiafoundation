The bill arrives. €300.

At the bottom, a line item: 70 percent of this covers salaries, labour, margins, fees,

Down the street there is a sign: cooking classes. €50. Bring your friends —
they join for free. You look through the window. The people inside are not professional
chefs. They are following a recipe. The food smells extraordinary.

Would you go back to the first restaurant? Or would you walk in?

The house costs €300,000. Same 70 percent line item — primarily salaries and labour for
the skilled professionals required at every stage of conventional construction, plus

€300,000 mortgage over thirty years generates approximately €150,000 to €180,000 in
interest. Total paid: €450,000 to €480,000. For a standard meal that took a year and
a half to prepare.

But most families with two median incomes cannot even get a table at that restaurant.
The bank will not approve the mortgage. The deposit is too large. The income multiple
does not qualify. So they eat from the street wagon — the run-down apartment, the
compromise neighbourhood, the house with someone else's problems. Or they go home
hungry. Back to their parents' basement. Waiting for a table that may never come.

That is restaurant one. Every restaurant in town. Same menu. Same prices. Same delay.
Same line item.

ABSTRACT
================================================================================

This thesis describes a complete family home — insulated, off-grid, finished, liveable —
built in one to two weeks, for approximately €50,000, with a screwdriver and a few friends.

The system uses EPAL Euro pallets as the ribbed core of a site-fabricated structural
composite panel — the RCP, Ribbed Composite Panel. CE-marked I-joists span the roof.
Standard 2x6 timber joists span the floor on earth screws. The result is a permanent,
insulated, certifiable family home that can be built in days, owned outright from day
one, and disassembled if circumstances require it.

A conventionally built 80 square metre family home in Western Europe costs approximately
€280,000 to €350,000 all in — construction, fees, permits, connections, VAT (Turner and
Townsend, 2025; BWS, 2026). The VAT alone on that build is approximately €50,000 to
€60,000. The complete pallet RCP house — shell, off-grid package, all professionals —
costs approximately €35,000 fully equipped. Less than the VAT on the conventional
alternative. At 80 square metres the system delivers living space at approximately
€437 per square metre — 8 to 10 times cheaper than the Western European median market
rate of €3,000 to €4,500 per square metre.

The single 145mm EPAL pallet wall filled with blown rockwool achieves approximately
0.39 to 0.42 W/m²K — 4 to 5 times better than standard European masonry at 1.8 W/m²K.
A double 290mm pallet wall achieves approximately 0.21 to 0.23 W/m²K — Passive House
territory. The wall panels operating at single storey carry well under 10 percent
of their theoretical compression capacity. The system is structurally overengineered for
single storey construction by a factor of approximately 200.

The primary limitation is the absence of a physical prototype. The RCP testing protocol
in Appendix H defines exactly how the prototype will be validated when built. Every
component in the system is independently certified for loads that far exceed what single
storey domestic construction demands. The prototype will confirm what overengineered
components already predict.

1. INTRODUCTION AND RESEARCH CONTEXT
================================================================================

1.1 The Housing Crisis — A Western World Problem

Across the western world, home ownership has become a privilege rather than a norm. In the
Netherlands, the average home cost approximately 488,000 euros in 2025 — an 11 percent
year-on-year increase — with the Dutch central bank confirming that house prices have risen
faster than borrowing capacity every year since 2013 (CBS, 2025; De Nederlandsche Bank, 2025).
In the major urban regions the average has already exceeded 500,000 euros. A generation ago,
Dutch baby boomers bought homes on a single average salary. Today, in most Dutch cities, two
people with above-average salaries cannot qualify for a mortgage on the average home.

The Netherlands is not exceptional. The same story plays out across the United Kingdom,
Australia, Canada, Germany, and Portugal. An entire generation — millennials and Generation Z —
has been priced out of the basic human act of owning the roof over their heads. Not because they
are less productive than their parents. Not because construction has become more complex. But
because the system that prices and delivers shelter has accumulated so many layers of
professional intermediaries, financial instruments, and embedded assumptions that the actual cost
of putting walls and a roof together has been obscured almost beyond recognition.

Verified market data for Western Europe in 2026 shows that an 80 square metre
conventionally built family home costs approximately €280,000 to €350,000 all in —
construction, fees, permits, utility connections, VAT, and the historical average
overrun (Turner and Townsend, 2025; BWS, 2026). In the United States the equivalent
figure is approximately $200,000 to $210,000. In either case the build takes 12 to 36
months.

1.2 What You Are Actually Paying For

A critical observation from conventional construction cost analysis is that materials represent
only 28 to 30 percent of total cost. The remaining 70 to 72 percent comprises architect and
project management fees, contractor margin, subcontractor markup, permits, surveys, and the
historical average cost of delays and overruns.

Building a house by conventional means requires the client to become the director of a large and
expensive orchestra — coordinating an architect, a project manager, a structural engineer, a
plumbing engineer, a drainage engineer, an acoustic engineer, a fire prevention engineer, an
electricity engineer, a main contractor, and multiple subcontractors. Between 50 and 100 people
may be involved in producing a building that one family will live in.

1.3 Conventional Construction in Portugal and Spain

In most of southern Europe, residential construction begins with a concrete slab. The slab
requires excavation, formwork, reinforcement steel, concrete delivery, curing time, and
specialist labour at every stage. It is permanent from the moment it is poured. It triggers
planning requirements in virtually every municipality. It cannot be moved, removed, or reversed.

In Portugal and Spain, the dominant construction method above the slab is a steel reinforced
concrete frame — columns and beams poured in place — with the cavities between structural
members filled with brick and mortar. The frame goes up slowly, column by column. The bricklayers
follow, filling the openings. Every trade waits for the previous trade to finish. Every delay
cascades. The building grows at a pace that seems, to anyone from a timber frame tradition,
incomprehensibly slow.

The result is a building that is thermally poor — a single layer of brick and mortar has a
U-value of approximately 1.8 W/m²K, compared to approximately 0.40 W/m²K for the
single pallet RCP wall — four to five times better insulated —
structurally rigid but unadaptable, expensive to heat, expensive to cool, and completely
permanent. It cannot be disassembled. It cannot be moved. It cannot be reused. When it reaches
the end of its life, it becomes rubble.

1.4 The Alternative — And Why It Is a Spectrum

The housing choice has collapsed into two extremes. At one end: a €300,000 to €500,000
conventional home, 30 years of debt, immovable, inflexible, owned by the bank. At the
other end: a tiny house in a forest, a campervan, an Amazon flatpack micro-cabin — cheap
but marginal and socially invisible. Between those two extremes there is almost nothing.

This system lives exactly in the middle. It is permanent but demountable. Certifiable but
buildable without permits. Mortgageable — a €50,000 home is trivially easy to finance if
you want to — but equally buildable outright for cash. It outperforms conventional
Portuguese masonry on thermal performance. It looks from the inside like any well-finished
home. Nobody sees the pallets.

And everything about it is a spectrum. Legal position is a spectrum. Foundation is a
spectrum. Labour is a spectrum — even if you hire a carpenter for assembly, a plumber,
an electrician, and an insulation contractor, the total cost remains a fraction of
conventional construction because the savings are built into the system, not only into
the self-build labour. Off-grid or municipal: both work. The only thing that is not a
spectrum is the panel dimension.

Leroy Merlin Portimao, like most large builders merchants across Europe, offers an
in-store cutting service. Timber lengths, OSB sheets, I-joists — all cut to specified
dimensions before leaving the store. Everything arrives on site already the right length.
The only operation remaining is assembly — connecting pre-certified components in a
defined sequence. Like flat-pack furniture: the sequence is self-evident, and when
finished there should be no leftover screws.

The complete structural shell of a 30 square metre house fits on a standard rental
flatbed trailer. Standard driving licence. Approximately 80 euros per day hire. A family
of four can unload everything in an hour. Assembly begins immediately.

1.5 Scope and Delimitations

This thesis is delimited to single-storey residential construction in the Algarve climate zone,
specifically the conditions of Lagos municipality: zero snow load, low wind load, 3,000 hours of
sunshine per year, mild winters, and dry summers. Adaptation to colder climates is discussed in
Chapter 19. The thesis does not present results from a physical prototype build. A physical
prototype is identified as the primary requirement for future research.

3. LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

3.1 SIP Panel Construction

Structural Insulated Panels were first developed in the United States in the 1930s and have been
in mainstream residential construction since the 1970s (SIPA, 2023). A SIP panel consists of a
rigid insulating core bonded between two structural skins, typically OSB. The composite action
of the bonded assembly produces structural performance significantly greater than the sum of the
individual components — a principle well established in structural engineering as composite beam
theory (Eurocode 5, EN 1995-1-1:2004).

OSB-faced panels with timber frame cores have been shown to achieve racking resistance
equivalent to conventional stud wall construction. Full-coverage structural PU adhesive
produces bond strengths that exceed the tensile strength of the OSB substrate itself —
making the bond stronger than the material it joins. The dominant core material in
commercial SIP production is expanded polystyrene — EPS foam — a petroleum-derived
plastic product that is highly flammable, releases toxic gases when burning, and is
non-biodegradable. Timber-core SIP variants are documented in Scandinavian timber frame
construction and represent the structural category into which the RCP system falls.

3.2 I-Joist Construction

Engineered wood I-joists have been in mainstream use for floor and roof construction in
Northern Europe and North America since the 1980s. STEICO, the largest European
manufacturer, documents that I-joists are 40 to 60 percent lighter than equivalent solid
timber sections while providing superior stiffness and dimensional stability (STEICO,
2023). The slim web section reduces thermal bridges compared to solid timber joists.
I-joists do not shrink, twist, or warp in service.

3.3 Alternative Material Construction

The use of alternative materials in construction has a documented base. The earthship
movement demonstrated the structural viability of earth-rammed tyres as building materials
(Reynolds, 2000). The WikiHouse project explored CNC-cut plywood as a self-build
structural system (Parvin et al., 2013). No peer-reviewed literature on EPAL pallet-core
composite wall panels within a two-component system combining site-assembled RCP walls
with CE-marked I-joist roof and floor was identified in the course of this research.
This absence represents the primary gap this thesis begins to address, and the most
significant opportunity for future research.

The principal barrier to adoption of alternative construction materials is not performance
but the absence of standardised testing data that engineers can reference. The approach
taken in this thesis — describing the pallet core as certified softwood timber infill
within an OSB-sheathed frame, using manufacturer-published I-joist span tables for floor
and roof — addresses this barrier within the constraints of current certification
frameworks.

3.4 Affordable and Self-Build Housing

Walter Segal's self-build system demonstrated that non-specialist builders could produce
structurally sound homes using simple timber frame methods and a modular design that eliminated
complex cutting (Broome and Richardson, 1991). The principle established was that simplification
of construction logic — not reduction of material quality — is the primary driver of cost
reduction in self-build. Community self-build programmes studied by Awan et al. (2011)
demonstrate that collective building effort produces higher quality outcomes than individual
self-build, because collective knowledge pools compensate for individual skill gaps.

3.5 Off-Grid Systems in Southern European Climates

The Algarve receives approximately 3,000 hours of sunshine per year and a global horizontal
irradiation of approximately 1,800 kWh per square metre per year (PVGIS, 2024). Fraunhofer ISE
(2023) reports that a south-facing 4kWp system in southern Portugal produces approximately 6,000
to 7,000 kWh annually. A 200-litre thermosiphon solar hot water system in southern
Portugal provides more than 80 percent of a four-person household's annual hot water
demand — a well-established performance figure for this climate zone.

3.6 Permaculture and Community Education Models

The permaculture design principle of finding productive uses for waste streams provides the
philosophical foundation for the educational model proposed in this thesis (Mollison and
Holmgren, 1978). Schon (1983) argues that real construction projects as educational settings
produce qualitatively different learning outcomes than controlled training environments, because
the presence of real stakes produces reflective rather than routine practice.

4. THE HOUSING CRISIS IN THE WESTERN WORLD

4.1 The Scale of the Problem

In the next twenty years, three billion people will need a new home. That is forty
percent of the world's current population. Today one in three people living in cities
lives in a slum. One hundred million people worldwide are homeless. The scale of the
building challenge facing humanity is civilisational — not a Western affordability
problem, not a planning problem, not a bureaucracy problem. A civilisational one.

The materials currently used to address this challenge are the wrong ones. Steel
represents approximately 3 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions. Concrete
represents over 5 percent. Together they account for approximately 8 percent of
humanity's total contribution to climate change (IEA, 2023). Cities built in these
two materials are cities built on a carbon foundation that the planet cannot sustain.

Wood is the only major building material grown by the power of the sun. It sequesters
carbon. It stores it. A timber building is a carbon bank, not a carbon debt. The RCP
system uses certified sustainable timber throughout — EPAL pallets from managed
European forestry, OSB from timber industry offcuts that would otherwise be waste.
Every RCP house built is a concrete-free, structural-steel-free structure that stores
carbon rather than emitting it.

There are approximately 500 million EPAL pallets in active European circulation today.
A complete 37.5m² house requires approximately 78 wall panels — approximately 100
pallets when ceiling, floor, and surplus panels are included. With 500 million pallets
in circulation, the existing supply could provide the raw material for approximately
5 million complete houses without manufacturing a single new pallet. The raw material
for millions of homes is already circulating in the European logistics network. Nobody
has to wait for materials. They exist. They are at the nearest builders merchant or
available from the nearest logistics depot today.

The data on Western housing affordability tells a consistent story regardless of the
country examined. In the Netherlands, the average home reached €488,000 in 2025, with
the Dutch central bank confirming that house prices have risen faster than borrowing
capacity every year since 2013 (De Nederlandsche Bank, 2025; CBS, 2025). In Australia,
the median home price in major cities exceeded one million Australian dollars in 2024.
In the United Kingdom, the average house price reached £285,000. In Canada, the
national average exceeded $700,000 Canadian dollars.

In Portugal, the average listed property price reached €436,000 in Q1 2026 — a 10.4
percent increase in a single year, significantly faster than both inflation and income
growth (Imovirtual, 2026). The proportion of properties listed below €300,000 fell from
32 percent to 28 percent in the same period. Affordable housing is not just expensive
in Portugal. It is disappearing as a market category.

In the Algarve, where this research is situated, the median property price reached
approximately €3,203 per square metre in Q3 2025 — a 16.6 percent year-on-year
increase (INE, 2025). A conventional contractor-built home costs approximately €1,500
to €2,500 per square metre to construct. Buying an existing home costs more.

The Portuguese construction industry has identified the solution. Paulo Lopes, CEO of
Casaiberia, writing in The Portugal News in May 2026: the problem is not a lack of
solutions, it is a lack of execution. The highest cost in construction is time lost to
bureaucracy. The answer is prefabrication and industrialisation — available now (Lopes,
2026). The RCP system is exactly that solution.

This system produces a fully equipped, off-grid square metre of living space for
approximately €333 at 73 square metres — nearly 10 times cheaper than the Algarve
secondary market. The variable cost of additional enclosed space is approximately €60
to €125 per square metre. At the Algarve market rate, the same square metre costs
€3,203.

The market is not just undersupplied — it is simultaneously being flooded with buyers.
Demand for homes in Portugal grew by 150.4 percent between February and April 2026
compared to the same period in 2025, with March recording 173.5 percent year-on-year
growth in property searches (Imovirtual, 2026b). Porto recorded a 214.6 percent annual
increase in searches in its main municipality alone. The person competing conventionally
for existing stock in 2026 is competing in a market where demand has more than doubled
in a single year while affordable supply has shrunk. The RCP builder exits this dynamic
entirely — they are not competing for existing stock. They are creating new stock at a
fraction of the market price.

The gap between €277 and €3,203 is not a gap in quality. It is a gap in knowledge.

4.1a The Mortgage as a Life Sentence

The conventional house comes with a mortgage. For most buyers that mortgage consumes a
significant fraction of household income for decades. In the United States in 2023,
mortgage payments consumed approximately 34 percent of median household income — the
highest level since the early 1980s (Bankrate, 2025). Nearly one in four new borrowers
spends more than 30 percent of income on mortgage payments — the standard affordability
stress threshold. In high-cost markets the share frequently exceeds 50 percent. To
qualify for a mortgage on the median-priced US home in 2025 requires a household income

5.1 The Structural Principle — One Idea, Two Applications

Every caravan and motorhome on European roads is built from expanded polystyrene foam
sandwiched between two thin sheets of aluminium or GRP. Foam with essentially zero
structural rigidity on its own. The structural strength comes entirely from the composite
action — two skins held apart by a core. This technology has been proven across millions
of units for decades. Nobody questions whether a caravan wall works.

A commercial SIP panel uses the same principle. It is a styrofoam Oreo. Two structural
cookies — the OSB skins — held apart by an inert foam filling. The composite action
transfers the structural work to the OSB skins. The foam core contributes almost nothing
to structural performance — it exists to hold the skins apart and provide insulation.
It is a petroleum product. It off-gasses. It burns with toxic smoke. It cannot be sourced
locally. It must be ordered from a factory that sets the price. There are 500 million
EPAL pallets in active European circulation and zero of them go into commercial SIPs.

The RCP is not a SIP with a stronger filling. It is a categorically different structural
concept.

The EPAL pallet is the internal skeleton. Certified structural timber in a precisely
defined three-dimensional geometry — stringers running vertically, deck boards running
horizontally, blocks at the corners. This skeleton has substantial structural capacity
in its own right. It resists compression. It has dimensional stability in three axes.

The OSB skins are the exoskeleton. Bonded continuously to every contact surface of the
internal skeleton with structural PU adhesive and ring shank nails, the OSB wraps the
timber core across its entire face. This is not point connection at discrete fasteners.
It is continuous bonded contact.

Under racking load — horizontal lateral force trying to push the top of the wall
sideways relative to the bottom — the OSB skin acts as a rigid diaphragm across its
entire face simultaneously. Load is distributed across every square centimetre of bonded
surface rather than concentrated at discrete fastener points as in a stud wall. The
racking resistance of an RCP panel is categorically superior to a stud wall of
equivalent dimensions — not marginally, categorically — because the mechanism of
resistance is fundamentally different.

Internal skeleton plus external exoskeleton. Both structural. Both bonded. Together they
produce a panel that is stronger, stiffer, and more racking-resistant than the sum of
its parts. This is the composite action that makes the system work.

Nothing wrong with stud walls. They are fast, well-understood, and widely certified.
The RCP panel is faster, cheaper, and structurally superior in racking resistance. That
is the case being made.

OSB was dismissed when introduced — it is just wood chips pressed together, how can that
be structural? Now nobody builds a timber frame house without it. Remove OSB from the
European and North American construction supply chain tomorrow and construction stops
overnight. Hospitals, schools, offices, homes — the structural frames of every timber
building built in the last thirty years depend on OSB. It is made from timber industry
offcuts and small-diameter logs that were previously waste. Compressed, glued, and
pressed into panels: the raw material is what the sawmill threw away, and the product
is what holds up the wall.

The I-joist was dismissed — a beam made from a thin web? Surely solid timber is stronger?
Now I-joists are standard in every serious timber frame project across Europe and North
America. They use 40 to 60 percent less timber than an equivalent solid section and
perform better in every measurable way — stiffer, straighter, lighter, more consistent.
The I-joist is itself made from OSB for the web section. Both products were dismissed.
Both won. Because the engineering is undeniable and the economics are compelling.

Both faced exactly the criticism being directed at the RCP system. Both won because the
numbers are undeniable. The EPAL pallet core is the next iteration of the same logic.

Take a single sheet of paper. It is completely flimsy — it folds under its own weight.
Now corrugate it into a wavy layer and sandwich it between two flat sheets. It becomes
cardboard — rigid, strong, and resistant to bending in all directions. The principle is
identical to what makes this system work. One OSB sheet is flimsy. Two OSB sheets with
a ribbed core bonded between them become an RCP — extraordinarily rigid and strong. The
bond between skin and core is what makes it structural. Neither element alone achieves
what the composite achieves.

The RCP panel has five strong components where a commercial SIP with foam core has two
strong components and one inert placeholder:

One — outer OSB3 15mm skin. CE marked, structural, dimensionally stable.
Two — inner OSB3 15mm skin. Same.
Three, four, five — three EPAL pallets side by side. Each certified to carry over a
tonne of dynamic load. EPAL certified softwood, heat treated to ISPM 15.

The nails and adhesive are not additional components. They are what turns five individual
players into one team. The adhesive transfers shear between all contact surfaces. The ring
shank nails provide mechanical connection through the full depth. The five components act
as one rigid ribbed composite unit.

The RCP and the I-joist are brothers. They are based on identical engineering logic. Both
place stiff material at the extremes — the OSB skins of the RCP, the timber flanges of
the I-joist — where bending stress is highest. Both use a lighter core to prevent the
stressed faces from buckling. The I-joist has been CE marked and used in mainstream
construction for decades. The commercial SIP has been CE marked and used for decades.
The RCP takes the same validated structural principle and substitutes one core material —
EPS petroleum foam — for another: EPAL certified softwood. This is not a structural
revolution. It is a logical material substitution using components that are already
validated, already certified, and already available off the shelf at any builders merchant.

Walk into any large builders merchant this afternoon, buy everything you need, and start
fabricating panels tomorrow. No custom orders. No lead times. No overseas imports. No
planning three months ahead. In some locations you can even borrow the earth screw
installation machine for free when you buy the screws. This system can be built
spontaneously. No other building system offers that.

Commercial SIP panels are genuinely structural and widely used. The RCP shares their
structural principle entirely. The key difference is not environmental or cost — though
both matter. The key difference is this: you cannot make a commercial SIP yourself. You
need a factory, a hydraulic press, and a lead time of weeks. The RCP is fabricated on
site in 15 to 20 minutes per panel with a jig, a nail gun, and materials from any
builders merchant. One system requires industrial infrastructure. The other requires a
flat surface and a morning.

The RCP is not a standard SIP. It is not yet certified. It is a new ribbed composite
system, defined by its geometry and validated through testing rather than assumption.

5.2 Component One: The RCP — Ribbed Composite Panel

5.2.1 The EPAL Euro Pallet — Graded Structural Infill

The EPAL Euro pallet is among the most precisely standardised timber assemblies in industrial
production. Manufactured to a single specification across all participating countries, each
certified unit consists of three 75x75mm softwood stringers spanning 1200mm, connected by nine
deck boards at 22mm nominal thickness, assembled with ring-shank mechanical fasteners and
structural adhesive, heat treated to ISPM 15, and inspected before certification. The resulting
assembly carries a certified dynamic load of 1,500 kg — over a tonne of impact, shock, and
abuse in any direction.

The dynamic load rating is the relevant figure for this system. A dynamic load is a moving,
impact load — the kind generated by a forklift dropping a pallet, by vibration in transit, by
sudden impact forces. The EPAL pallet is engineered to absorb that kind of punishment
repeatedly without failure. Three of these bonded and nailed between two OSB skins produce a
panel that is practically indestructible during the construction process. During fabrication,
during loading onto a trailer, during transport to site, during erection — the panel can be
handled roughly, stacked, slid, bumped, and it does not care. You are not handling a fragile
component. You are handling something that was designed to be abused by industrial machinery.

For the purposes of this system, the pallet is presented as graded, certified softwood
structural infill with defined geometry and known properties — the EPAL standard.

Not every pallet is accepted. A simple, fast, repeatable grading protocol is applied:

TYPE CONTROL: Only EPAL/EUR certified pallets are accepted for wall panels. The EPAL stamp
confirms baseline quality and dimensional consistency.

REJECTION CRITERIA: Reject if any stringer shows a split exceeding approximately 50 percent of
its depth, if major chunks of stringer material are missing, if visible rot or soft degraded
wood is present, or if severe twist prevents the pallet from lying flat within tolerance. Accept
pallets with cosmetic damage, cracked deck boards, or minor surface defects that do not affect
the primary stringer assembly.

MECHANICAL PRE-SCREENING: Support the pallet at its ends and stand on the centre. This is
not a structural test — it is a five-second pre-screening check that catches obviously
compromised units before they enter the full grading protocol. A pallet that fails under
body weight has failed catastrophically and would have been rejected by visual inspection
anyway. The actual quality assurance is the EPAL certification, the visual grading, the
moisture check, and the peel test on the finished panel.

MOISTURE CONTROL: Measure with a pin moisture meter on the deck board surface — not the
stringer. The stringer moisture content is irrelevant because no adhesive contacts it. Target
below 15 percent on deck board surface before fabrication. EPAL pallets manufactured to
specification have maximum 22 percent moisture at manufacture. Most EPAL pallets in the
Algarve will already be well below 15 percent on the deck board surface after outdoor storage.
A €15 moisture meter is the only instrument required for this step.

This protocol eliminates the weak tail of material variation and creates an engineer-readable
quality control process. The system does not depend on the perfection of any single pallet. It
depends on the controlled exclusion of the genuinely defective ones.

Approximately 500 million EPAL pallets are in active European circulation at any given time.

THE MUTUAL RESTRAINT PRINCIPLE: The structural role of the pallet in this system is more
than passive core. The OSB skins hold the pallets in place laterally — preventing them from
shifting or rotating. The pallets hold the OSB skins in place — preventing them from buckling
under axial load. They are mutually restraining. This is composite action: the bonded assembly
is stronger than the sum of its parts because each element prevents the other from failing in
its critical mode.

Foam in a commercial SIP panel restrains the OSB but contributes nothing to the composite
stiffness. The EPAL pallet core actively contributes to both restraint and stiffness through
the stringer assembly. The main task of the pallet is holding the OSB in place. The reverse is
equally true — the OSB is holding the pallet in place. Since all three pallets are stacked and
held in position by the OSB on both faces, they act as a structural assembly rather than as
three independent elements. Three pallets alone might shift and rotate. Three pallets bonded
between two OSB skins act as a single rigid ribbed composite unit.

The pallet performs the same structural role as the foam core in a commercial SIP panel — and
then some. It is a lateral restraint that also contributes structural depth. The I-joist and
the RCP panel are based on identical logic: two stiff faces held apart by a core that prevents
buckling. In both cases, neither element works without the other. The bond between them is what
makes the composite structural.

5.2.2 The OSB Facing — The Bread of the Sandwich

OSB is manufactured almost entirely from what the timber industry previously discarded:
small-diameter logs, crooked trunks, branches and offcuts too irregular for conventional sawn
timber. Chips and strands plus adhesive and pressure produce a certified structural panel used
in construction programmes worldwide. The raw material is waste. The product is exceptional.

What makes OSB structurally exceptional is that it is, in the most useful sense, dead wood.
Unlike sawn timber, which shrinks and expands continuously with changes in ambient humidity,
OSB is dimensionally stable. The manufacturing process eliminates the hygroscopic response of
the wood fibres. The panel does not move.

This stability is accompanied by exceptional in-plane shear strength. A sheet of OSB fixed to
a frame acts as a structural diaphragm, resisting racking forces across its full face
simultaneously. Four OSB-sheathed walls connected at their corners form a structural box —
self-stabilising as it is erected, remaining precisely as built for the life of the structure.

The sheet dimension — 2500mm by 1250mm, exactly two to one — rationalises with reference to
the EPAL Euro pallet. Three pallets at 800mm each produce 2,400mm of wall height. An OSB sheet
is 2,500mm tall. The 100mm differential is resolved by the double 2x4 composite top plate. OSB
arrives at every construction site in Europe delivered on Euro pallets. The two products were
designed, independently, to work together. This is not coincidence — it is the consequence of
the same global logistics standardisation system converging on compatible dimensions over fifty
years.

5.2.5 Panel Fabrication — The Assembly Site Principle

This is not a build site. It is an assembly site.

A build site is where things are made from raw materials requiring skilled judgment at
every step. An assembly site is where pre-made, pre-certified, pre-finished components
are connected in a defined sequence. Every time-consuming operation — fabrication,
painting, cutting, drying, curing — happens before the assembly day, at a pace that
fits around normal life. The assembly day is pure connection.

THE COMPLETE PRE-FABRICATION SEQUENCE:

Weeks, months, or even a year before assembly day — ground screws installed by the
hydraulic machine operator. The foundation sits waiting. No curing. No maintenance.
Ready whenever.

In weekends and evenings before assembly day — RCP panels fabricated one or two at a
time on the jig. Each cures fully. Salt poured into base. Inner OSB installed. Gypsum
screwed on flat. Joints filled and dried. Limewash applied in two coats, each fully
dry. Pre-painted exterior cladding boards cut to 2500mm with a 45-degree mitre at each
end — one cut per 5-metre board, no measuring, stop-saw set once — then brad-nailed
vertically to the outer OSB face. The 45-degree mitre at the board base points outward
and downward, directing water away from the end grain. The angled cut is made on the
stop-saw and produces two identical boards from one length — zero waste, zero measuring.

Electrical holes pre-drilled at 300mm from bottom, 300mm from right edge of each panel.
This is the socket hole and the inspection hatch — the same hole serving both purposes.
In normal use it is covered by a flush plastic cap painted to match the wall finish,
invisible. To install a wall socket: remove the cap, route EMT conduit through the
existing hole, install the socket plate over it. To inspect the wall cavity: remove
the cap, insert moisture meter probe, phone camera, or simply smell. One hole. One cap.
Two functions. Window and door openings cut while panel is flat. Frames fitted. Every
panel stacked under a tarpaulin, fully finished, fully cured, waiting.

Steel roof sheets ordered in standard commercial lengths — 5.0m, 5.5m, 6.0m, 6.5m,
or 7.0m — corrugated or trapezoidal profiled steel with pre-bonded condensation mat
on underside. The house dimension is chosen to match a standard sheet length exactly.
No cutting required. No angle grinder. No ragged cut edge. Order at length. Install
at length.

I-joists ordered to length. Floor joists cut and stored. All materials on site and
ready before assembly day begins.

Make 5 to 15 surplus panels during fabrication. The marginal cost per additional panel
is low — materials only, no additional jig setup. Use the best panels for the house.
Any panel slightly off-square or cosmetically imperfect becomes a shed, a playhouse,
a workshop wall, a chicken house. Nothing is wasted. Nothing is discarded.

THE FLAT FABRICATION PRINCIPLE:

Everything that can be done to a panel while it is horizontal should be done while it
is horizontal. Paint applied horizontally produces no drips and no runs — dramatically
better results with essentially zero skill. Brad nails driven into a horizontal surface
go in straight and consistent. Gypsum joints filled flat feather perfectly. Children
can contribute to the painting team. The panel is lifted complete: exterior cladded and
painted, interior gypsum limewashed, electrical hole pre-drilled, inspection hatch
fitted, salt in base. The erection crew lifts a finished wall, not a shell.

THE THREE-TEAM BUILD:

The build runs three parallel teams simultaneously, not sequentially.

ASSEMBLY TEAM: Fabricates RCP cores — outer OSB down, pallets bonded and nailed,
ratchet straps clamping, cure. As cured panels come off the jig, the same team adds
salt to the base, installs inner OSB, drills the standardised electrical hole, screws
gypsum board flat, tapes joints and fills the 3mm tapered edge recess with joint
compound. Erects panels when ready. Installs I-joists and roof.

PAINT TEAM: Runs simultaneously from the start. Cuts exterior cladding boards to
2500mm — one cut each on a stop-saw set to length, no measuring. Pre-paints all
cladding boards lying flat on trestles — rolling paint on a horizontal surface,
no drips, no skill required. Pre-paints and limewashes gypsum boards before they go
onto panels. Whitewashes or pine-tar treats ceiling pallets lying flat. Paints I-joist
flanges. Brad-nails pre-painted cladding to outer OSB face of each cured panel before
erection — vertical boards, shadow gap spacer, two nails per board top and bottom.
Applies limewash to pre-installed gypsum while panel is still flat.

FOUNDATION TEAM: Installs earth screws simultaneously. Sets high-side brackets
250 to 350mm higher than low-side brackets for mono-pitch roof drainage. Cuts and
treats sill beams. Foundation is ready when the first panels are ready.

Rate-limiting step: RCP core adhesive cure — approximately 3 to 4 hours to handling
strength at Algarve temperatures with standard Sikaflex 512. For a 24-hour build,
specify Sika Fast-5215 NT or equivalent fast-cure two-component structural PU —
handling strength in approximately 15 to 30 minutes, full cure in 2 hours. Higher
cost per panel, justified for speed-critical builds.

WALL JACK AND PLUMB LASER — ERECTION WITHOUT LADDERS:

All RCP panels for one full wall are assembled horizontally on the ground alongside
the sill beam — connected to each other and to the double top plate while flat. I-joist
hangers are pre-fixed to the top plate at 625mm centres while everything is still on
the ground. The complete wall assembly — panels, top plate, hangers — lies flat next
to where it will stand.

Two wall jacks — pole jacks, QualCraft-style or equivalent, available for approximately
€50 to €150 per pair — clamp onto short 2x6 posts hinged at the sill beam. The ratchet
mechanism raises the complete wall from horizontal to vertical in minutes. One person
can operate this alone. Two people is faster. No ladder required. No team of six
pushing and shouting.

A cross laser set to vertical mode — available for €25 to €80 — projects a vertical
line up the face of the wall in real time during the raise. The entire team can see
simultaneously whether the wall is plumb. No climbing. No spirit level at height. When
the laser line aligns with the reference mark on the sill beam: plumb. Lock the
temporary brace. Move to the next wall.

ERECTION SEQUENCE — BATHROOM CORNER FIRST:

Always begin erection at the bathroom corner. The wet wall goes up first. As soon as
the bathroom corner panels are tied together, the interior team immediately begins
bathroom fit-out — tiling, toilet, sink, shower. This is the longest interior task.
Starting it on the first day of erection rather than after the full shell is complete
saves significant time. The kitchen shares the wet wall back-to-back. The plumber
connects both from one location on one visit.

Two walls connected at a corner — structurally stable, temporarily braced. Roof team
begins placing I-joists on the completed top plate. Three walls up — interior team
has been working for hours. Fourth wall going up — roof closes over the completed
section simultaneously. By the time the fourth wall is complete the house is
weathertight and the bathroom is partially fitted.

different cost proposition from conventional construction supervision. One day. One
fee. No delays. The professional is not building — they are advising. They confirm

Cost: one day's professional rate. The most valuable professional expenditure in the

What gets documented: pallet inspection photographs, adhesive application before each

A SALTY PEST INSURANCE — THREE LEVELS:

Termites and carpenter ants are the primary threat to any timber structure in warm
climates. In the Algarve, Reticulitermes grassei termites cause significant structural
damage to unprotected timber buildings every year. Carpenter ants hollow out timber
from the inside, invisible until the damage is done. Wood-boring beetles work slowly
and silently through untreated timber over years. Every one of these organisms requires
moisture to establish and thrive. Every one of them is killed by direct contact with
concentrated salt.

No biological organism — insect, fungus, mould, bacteria, or rodent — will willingly
enter a salt environment. And any that do will not survive it. Salt has been the
universal biological barrier for five thousand years. It does not wear off. It does not
need renewal. It does not require maintenance.

The other fear — the one anyone who has lived in an old house knows — is the dead mouse
in the wall. The smell that starts faint and becomes unbearable. The wall you have to
open to find the source. The repair. The memory. With salt in the base of every wall
cavity, a mouse that enters and dies is mummified rather than decomposed. No smell. No
damage. No wall demolition. No surveyor's report mentioning rodent evidence when you
come to sell.

This is A Salty Pest Insurance. Salt in the base of every wall panel. Salt in the base
of every floor joist bay. Optionally, a salt layer in the foundation bed under the
entire house for complete perimeter protection. The cost is negligible — a bag of
coarse marine salt costs almost nothing, and near the coast you can source it for
essentially free. One bulk order covers all three levels.

THE MOISTURE MECHANISM — CORRECTLY STATED:

Salt is hygroscopic — it actively draws moisture out of surrounding materials. Any
moisture present in the timber at the base of the wall is pulled into the salt. The
timber stays drier than it would be without the salt present. In the event of a
significant water leak reaching the wall base, the salt absorbs and retains the water
rather than allowing it to spread through the timber. Salt-impregnated timber is
preserved timber. The salt acts as protection in dry conditions by keeping moisture
away from the wood, and as preservation in wet conditions by impregnating the wood
with brine that inhibits all biological activity.

The reason the salt is placed only at the base of the wall — not packed through the
full cavity — is thermal. A wall cavity packed with salt would have no insulation
value. A 100mm layer at the base provides all the biological protection with no
meaningful impact on the wall's thermal performance.

LEVEL ONE — PANEL BASES:
100mm of coarse marine salt poured into the base of every RCP panel cavity before
the inner OSB closes. Permanent. Zero maintenance. Termites entering from below
encounter salt before they encounter timber. Carpenter ants entering through ground
level find a hostile environment at every wall base. Any moisture is drawn away from
the timber automatically.

LEVEL TWO — FLOOR CAVITY:
30mm of coarse marine salt across the base of every floor joist bay before insulation
goes in. The floor void — the space most vulnerable to ground moisture and ground-level
pest entry — is permanently protected.

LEVEL THREE — FOUNDATION BED (OPTIONAL):
Gravel base, sand layer, salt layer on top. The entire underside of the house sits on
a permanently sterile surface. Nothing biological establishes itself under the house.
Combined with perimeter drainage to prevent salt runoff into natural watercourses.

WET WALL SALT RESERVOIR:
Before closing the wet wall panels, coat the bottom 300mm of the internal cavity
surfaces with a waterproof membrane — bituminous paint or EPDM liquid rubber. Fill
to 100mm depth with salt above the membrane. Any plumbing leak flows into the salt
reservoir. The water is absorbed. The timber is preserved rather than damaged. A
plumbing fault can remain undetected for months without structural consequence.

STANDARDISED ELECTRICAL HOLE — SOCKET AND INSPECTION HATCH IN ONE:

Every panel receives a pre-drilled hole at exactly 300mm from the bottom edge and
300mm from the right edge — bottom right corner, consistent across every panel. The
hole serves two purposes simultaneously. In normal use: a flush plastic cap painted to
match the wall finish sits in the hole, invisible. To install a wall socket: remove
the cap, route EMT conduit through the existing hole, install the socket plate over
it. To inspect the wall cavity: remove the cap, insert a moisture meter probe, a
phone camera, or simply smell. Same hole. Same cap. Socket or inspection port depending
on what you need.

The hole is drilled through the gypsum, inner OSB, and into the pallet structure —
positioned to clip the edge of one deck board rather than cut through a stringer.
The deck board edge contribution to the hole is negligible given the panel's massive
structural overcapacity.

The hole position is marked on the jig. Drill if the panel needs a socket or
inspection point — which is every habitable panel. Every panel where a socket is
needed has the hole in exactly the same place. The electrician arrives at a house
where every outlet location is pre-determined, every hole is pre-drilled, and every
EMT conduit route is pre-planned.

SCREW ANYWHERE — THE 50 PERCENT TIMBER WALL:

The RCP wall is approximately 50 percent solid timber by cross section. A screw driven
anywhere through the gypsum and inner OSB will hit solid pallet timber within 15mm —
no stud finding required, no hollow spots, no failed fixings at random positions.
Every fixing point on every wall is structural. Kitchen cabinets, wall-mounted
shelving, washing machine brackets, bathroom vanity units, floating bed frames, the
television — all hung from the wall at any position with standard structural screws.

This enables the wall-mounted everything philosophy completely. Kitchen units float.
The washing machine hangs from the wall with clearance below. Everything the robot
vacuum needs to reach underneath is reachable. The floor goes from wall to wall
completely uninterrupted — visually larger, permanently cleanable, no furniture legs
to stub a toe on.

THE HOLLOW WALL — THE REAL ADVANTAGE:

The pre-drilled hole is not what makes this system extraordinary for electrical
installation. The hollow wall is.

There are no studs. There is no blocking. There is no timber obstructing any path
inside the wall cavity from floor to ceiling, left to right, diagonally — in any
direction. The pallet void is a generous open channel that runs the full height and
length of every panel. EMT conduit — rigid steel tube — can be pushed through loose
rockwool insulation in any direction without drilling, without fishing, without
specialist tools. One person pushes the conduit. It goes where you point it.

This makes the electrical installation fast, cheap, and — crucially — future-proof.
Every 1250mm along every wall there is a potential socket location at a known position:
300mm from the bottom right corner of every panel. If you want an additional socket
anywhere in the house — after the house is built, furnished, and lived in for five
years — you know exactly where to look. Knock a small hole in the gypsum at the known
position. Push a length of stiff conduit through the cavity to the nearest existing
conduit run. The electrician connects the wire. One hour of work at €25 per hour.

No jackhammer. No chasing channels in plaster. No replastering. No repainting. No
disruption to the existing interior beyond one small hole and one small patch.

Even with loose rockwool blown in, the stiff EMT conduit can be pushed through the
wall after the fact. The insulation compresses around it and springs back. The wall
accepts new conduit runs as easily after ten years as on the day it was built.

EMT CONDUIT ROUTING — AFTER ERECTION:

Once panels are erected, EMT conduit is routed by passing it through the pre-drilled
holes and through the pallet cavity. Vertical runs go straight up the panel height
inside the void. Horizontal runs travel along the void between stringer positions.
The pallet geometry creates a natural conduit grid — horizontal channels every 300mm,
vertical channels at every panel joint. One person routes the entire house without
specialist skill. The electrician connects the wires. One day at €25 per hour.
Dramatically less labour than any conventional electrical installation.

CEILING SPOTLIGHTS — PLANNED DURING BUILD:

One-time lightweight export pallets — full deck, not EPAL — used as ceiling panels
between I-joist bays have natural gaps between their deck boards. Standard LED
downlight housings — 70mm to 80mm diameter — fit directly into these gaps without
drilling or cutting. The spotlight drops in from below. The wiring runs above the
pallet board surface before the rockwool goes in.

Plan spotlight positions before the roof closes. At the chosen positions, drop a
length of conduit through the pallet gap before the rockwool is installed. The
conduit hangs below the ceiling ready for connection. The rockwool goes in above,
covering and securing the conduit above ceiling level. When the electrician arrives,
the conduit is already routed. Fit the downlight housing from below. Connect.

The entire ceiling lighting system is installed before the insulation goes in —
while everything is fully accessible from above. The result after roof closure: a
finished ceiling of pallet boards with flush LED downlights in the gaps between
boards, wired and ready. Zero overhead work. Zero scaffolding. Zero second visits.

5.2.6 Panel Fabrication — Complete Step Protocol

PRODUCTION RATE: Approximately 15 to 20 minutes per panel with a jig and an experienced
team. Three to four panels per hour is achievable. Manufacturing is the careful precision
phase. Assembly is the fast phase. Keep them separate.

FABRICATION CONDITIONS: Still air. Dry. No wind. Morning preferred — lower temperature,
longer adhesive open time. Sikaflex 512 open time varies with temperature and humidity —
consult the product data sheet for specific conditions. In Algarve morning conditions the
open time is comfortably long. Do not apply adhesive and then stop for any reason before
nailing. Apply, place, nail — one continuous operation.

FABRICATION SURFACE: Must be flat — concrete slab, compacted gravel, or scaffold boards.
Check with a long straight edge. Deviation greater than 5mm across the panel length is
unacceptable. A panel that cures on an uneven surface cures with a bow.

THE JIG: A full-size fabrication jig is built once and reused for every panel. The jig
marks pallet spacer block positions, defines nail positions, ensures consistent spacing,
and marks the standardised electrical hole position at 300mm x 300mm from bottom right
corner. The jig eliminates missed fasteners, standardises quality, and removes skill
dependency from every repeated operation. Build the jig before fabricating the first
panel. It is the single most important quality assurance tool in the system.

STEP 1: Lay OSB outer skin flat on fabrication surface, contact face up.

STEP 2: Wire brush or 60 to 80 grit sandpaper across the full contact face of the OSB.
Breaks through the factory phenol-formaldehyde resin surface layer, exposing raw strand
structure and increasing adhesive penetration. Blow off all dust immediately.

STEP 3: Light water mist on the sanded OSB face. Visibly damp, not wet. Activates PU
moisture cure from the OSB side on contact.

STEP 4: Grade and position three pallets on the OSB using the jig as guide — mid-side
orientation, 800mm height, 1200mm width, 145mm depth. Pallets placed edge to edge
spanning the 2500mm OSB length. Jig marks confirm spacer block positions.

STEP 5: Stiff bristle brush across all pallet deck board surfaces. Remove all dust and
debris.

STEP 6: Apply two 300ml cartridges of Sikaflex 512 to pallet deck board surfaces — not
to OSB. Parallel beads at approximately 60mm spacing across all deck board contact zones.
Every deck board receives adhesive. Apply to pallets because the contact zones are visible
and defined.

STEP 7: Two people lower OSB onto pallets. Align before contact. Lower straight down.
No sliding.

STEP 8 — NAIL GUN IMMEDIATELY. Two nail types, two purposes:

STRUCTURAL NAILS — 90mm ring shank: Driven into the pallet spacer blocks (the thick
timber blocks at the corners and centre of the pallet). Two nails per spacer block, nine
spacer blocks per pallet, 27 spacer fixings per panel total. These nails anchor deep into
solid wood and provide the primary mechanical connection. The spacer blocks are the
strongest timber in the pallet — this is where the load transfers.

SURFACE NAILS — 50mm ring shank: Applied across deck board zones between spacer blocks.
These distribute clamping force, prevent local separation between fastener points, and
support adhesive bonding across the full deck board surface.

Apply all nails immediately after OSB contact — one continuous operation. The jig marks
the spacer positions so every nail hits solid wood.

STEP 9 — RATCHET STRAPS: Loop two or three heavy duty ratchet straps around the complete
panel — over the OSB face, under the pallet assembly. Place a 2x4 or 2x6 timber spreader
along each edge between the strap and the OSB face to distribute strap pressure evenly
and prevent localised crushing at the strap contact point. A soft rubber or foam interface
between spreader and OSB protects the OSB surface. Tighten straps firmly — even and
distributed pressure across the full panel. Do not over-compress — excessive pressure can
squeeze adhesive out of the bond line and reduce performance.

FOR PROFESSIONAL BUILDS: Nails plus ratchet straps is the complete clamping system.
No screws required. Clamping is handled by the straps. Structural behaviour is governed
by the adhesive composite action and the nail mechanical connection.

FOR DIY BUILDS: Add screws after nailing — 50mm Spax at 300mm centres across all deck
board contact zones. Screws provide additional sustained tension clamping during cure and
increase tolerance to any execution variability in adhesive coverage or nail placement.
Screws are a process safety measure for DIY builds, not a structural necessity.

STEP 10: Slide panel to stack. OSB face down on previous panel's OSB face — clean faces
together, pallet assemblies outward and visible for inspection throughout cure. Each panel
weighs approximately 90kg. Eight panels stacked generates approximately 720kg of
distributed clamping pressure on the bottom panel. Stack on flat solid base. Sandbags
on top of the stack add further distributed pressure if desired.

Cure minimum 24 hours before erection.

5.2.6 Batch Quality Control — The Peel Test

One panel per fabrication batch is designated the test panel. After cure, cut a 150 x
150mm strip from its edge. Screw a timber block to the OSB face. Pry apart until failure.

PASS: OSB wood fibres tear before the bond line peels. Bond is stronger than the timber.
Batch accepted.

FAIL: Bond line peels cleanly. Stop. Do not erect the batch. Identify the cause:

Deck board moisture above 15 percent — re-dry, verify, re-fabricate.
Contaminated surfaces — brush protocol not followed — re-fabricate with correct preparation.
Insufficient adhesive — increase to three cartridges, reduce bead spacing to 50mm.

Every exposed timber surface that will be visible in the finished building is treated
before it goes up. All painting happens flat on the ground. No overhead painting ever.
While two people erect the roof structure, a third whitewashes the one-time pallets on
the ground. Ceiling is finished before it becomes a ceiling.

5.3 Component Two: The I-Joist

5.3.1 What an I-Joist Is

An engineered wood I-joist consists of two solid timber flanges — top and bottom — connected by
a thin web of OSB or fibreboard. The cross-section resembles the capital letter I. The flanges
are positioned where bending stress is highest — at the top and bottom of the beam — and the
web carries almost no bending stress, made from the minimum material necessary to resist shear.

A solid 2x6 rafter wastes most of its material on the unstressed middle zone. An I-joist puts
timber only where the stress is. The result is a beam that is 40 to 60 percent lighter than
solid timber of equivalent structural depth, significantly stiffer, and capable of spanning
greater distances. Dimensionally stable — I-joists do not shrink, twist, or warp in service.

Standard STEICO I-joists are available in depths of 160mm, 200mm, 220mm, and 240mm in standard
6-metre lengths from builders merchants across Europe. They are CE marked, carry manufacturer
span tables covering all standard domestic load cases, and can be specified directly in an
engineer's submission by reference to published data.

5.3.2 Why I-Joists for This System

The I-joist is the appropriate choice for floor and roof for three reasons. First, its structural
performance at standard domestic spans is fully documented and engineer-verifiable without any
additional testing. Second, the cavity depth of a 200mm I-joist produces a roof and floor
assembly of exceptional thermal performance. Third, it is light enough to be handled by two
people without mechanical assistance.

5.3.3 Making Your Own I-Joists — Floor Only, Informal Builds

For informal builds where engineer certification of the floor is not required, I-joists can be
fabricated on site. Two 38x63mm solid timber lengths, a router with a straight bit and fence
guide, strips of 9mm OSB, and structural PU adhesive are all that is required.

A homemade 200mm I-joist costs approximately 5 euros per 6-metre length. A bought STEICO
equivalent costs 18 to 22 euros.

This option is appropriate for the floor only — and only in informal builds where no engineer
certification is required. For the roof, CE-marked I-joists with manufacturer span tables are
always specified, regardless of legal position. The roof carries wind uplift, dynamic loads,
and long-term creep that require certified performance data. There is no DIY alternative for
the roof structure.

5.4 Why This System Costs What It Costs

The cost reduction achieved by this system is not the result of one clever trick. It is the
result of four simultaneous levers applied at once.

The first lever is materials. Conventional construction in the Algarve allocates 28 to 30
percent of total cost to materials — concrete, brick, tile, reinforcement steel, timber. This
system replaces the most expensive of those with EPAL pallets sourced at bulk price and OSB
sheet from a builders merchant. The effective material cost for the structural wall system is
reduced by more than 50 percent against conventional equivalents.

The second lever is complexity. A conventional build requires an architect, a project manager,
a structural engineer, a plumbing engineer, a drainage engineer, a main contractor, and multiple
subcontractors. Each layer adds margin, coordination cost, and overhead. This system requires
three licensed professionals for sign-off only — structural engineer, electrician, plumber.
Every other role is performed by the owner. The orchestration layer is eliminated entirely.

The third lever is risk. In conventional construction, the contractor carries the risk of
overruns, delays, and defects — and prices accordingly. That risk premium is embedded in every
quote. In this system, the owner carries the risk. The owner is also the person most motivated
to manage it well, because the consequences are entirely their own.

The fourth lever is labour. The owner does the work. Not because they are a builder, but
because the system is designed so that they do not need to be. Pre-cut components. No on-site
measurement. A defined sequence that is self-evident at every step. The skill required is
attention and patience — both available in abundance to anyone motivated enough to build their
own home.

Applied together, these four levers move the total cost of a complete, fully equipped,
off-grid, 80 square metre family home from approximately €280,000 to €350,000 by
conventional Western European means to approximately €35,000 by this system. The
difference is not a rounding error. It is a different philosophy of how a house gets
built.

Consider this: in Portugal, the government has reduced VAT on affordable housing
construction to 6 percent — acknowledging that the crisis demands intervention. At
6 percent, the VAT on a €300,000 Portuguese build is approximately €18,000. The
complete RCP structural shell — earth screws, panels, roof, insulation, windows and
doors — costs approximately €15,000 to €18,000. The weathertight insulated shell
costs less than or equal to the tax. For the price of the whole conventional build's
VAT at standard Western European rates of 20 percent — approximately €50,000 to
€60,000 — you can build the entire finished house using this system: fully insulated,
fully off-grid, mortgage-free. The ignorance tax, made literal.

5.5 The Orchestra of Complexity — And Why the Best Professionals Cost Less

Conventional construction is not a building process. It is an orchestra. Architect,
structural engineer, contractor, fifteen trades, each playing their own instrument,
none of them looking at the full score. The conductor — the project manager — spends
most of their time managing the gap between what was written and what is being played.
Every instrument adds cost. Every additional player adds another opportunity for the
performance to go wrong. The audience — the client — pays for the orchestra whether
it plays well or badly.

The RCP system is not a simpler orchestra. It is a different kind of music entirely.
Three certified components. A jig. A protocol. No conductor required.

THE SKILLED LABOUR PARADOX:

Conventional construction requires skilled labour in theory. The architect designs precisely.
The engineer calculates meticulously. The specification is detailed. In practice, the person
executing the work is rarely the person the specification assumed. A licensed contractor
typically means one person with a licence and a crew of helpers — some experienced, some not,
none of them incentivised to exceed the minimum standard required to pass inspection.

The helpers are paid by the day. They are not paid by the quality of the outcome. The fastest
route through each task is always preferred over the most correct route. Details the engineer
marked as critical are skipped not through malice but through time pressure, lack of
understanding, or indifference to outcomes they will never see. Insulation is compressed.
Vapour barriers are torn and not repaired. Fixings are wrong. The concrete is poured slightly
off-level. The gap between what was designed and what was built is often significant and always
invisible once the walls are closed.

The more complex the system, the more opportunities for error. Fifty steps give fifty chances
to get something wrong. A complex junction detail that took the engineer an afternoon to design
takes the worker four minutes to execute incorrectly — and nobody ever knows.

This is not a criticism of tradespeople. It is a criticism of complexity. The solution is not
better workers. The solution is fewer steps. If the protocol has five steps and each step is
self-evident, the margin for error approaches zero regardless of who is doing the work. A
motivated non-professional following five clear steps consistently outperforms a skilled
tradesperson executing fifty complex ones — not because the non-professional is more capable,
but because the simpler system gives them less room to fail.

MISTAKES ARE CHEAP AND REVERSIBLE:

When a mistake is made in conventional concrete construction it is frequently permanent.
A wall cast slightly out of plumb stays out of plumb. A foundation poured off level is
corrected expensively or not at all. The mistake is buried and the house is built on top of it.

In this system, mistakes are cheap and trivially reversible. If a panel is assembled
incorrectly — wrong nail pattern, adhesive not properly applied, structural concern identified
after cure — remove it. The panels unscrew. The pallets separate from the adhesive with a pry
bar. New materials cost almost nothing. A replacement panel is fabricated in the same time as
the original. The mistake costs an afternoon, not a demolition.

The components are so cheap that replacing a flawed panel is genuinely a minor event. In a
€300,000 conventional build, a mistake in the foundation or frame can cost tens of thousands
to rectify. In an RCP build, the equivalent mistake costs €200 in materials and one afternoon
of work.

LOCALISED DAMAGE — THE COMPARTMENT PRINCIPLE:

If a car drives through a wall of a conventional masonry house, the structural damage can be
catastrophic. A masonry wall is a monolithic system — breaking one section affects the
integrity of adjacent sections. Repairs require structural temporary works, partial demolition,
and specialist rebuilding.

If a car drives through an RCP wall, three panels are damaged. Unscrew the damaged panels.
Fabricate three replacements on the same jig. Erect them. Reconnect. One day of work.
Approximately €400 in materials. The rest of the house is completely unaffected because the
panels are modular and the damage is contained to the panels that were struck.

The same principle applies to any localised damage — fire in one corner, water damage from a
roof leak, subsidence under one earth screw. In each case the affected panels are removed and
replaced. The rest of the house stands undisturbed. The RCP system is self-repairing at
component level in a way no monolithic construction can be.

Individual builders can still be honest. A sole trader has a reputation to protect in a
community where word travels fast. The deeper problem is that even an honest builder is not
building your house as if they will live in it. There is a direct relationship between personal
investment in an outcome and the care given to details nobody inspects. The gap between what
a person builds for themselves and what someone builds for a fee is not a quality of character.
It is a quality of consequence.

THE PREMIUM PROFESSIONAL ADVANTAGE:

When the structural shell costs €35,000 instead of €300,000, you can afford the best
professionals because you only need them for one day each. The best plumber's hourly rate
is irrelevant when the total engagement is eight hours. The best electrician for one day
costs the same as a mediocre one — and delivers something completely different.

The best professionals do not just execute what you tell them. They say: why don't you
just do this instead. Simpler. Cheaper. Faster. More elegant. A pre-consultation with the
electrician before the build — one hour — walks through your panel layout and produces a

certify. Two visits. One day total. The best version of their knowledge, not the tired
version under time pressure.

The blow-in rockwool contractor is the model: specialist equipment, every cavity filled
in one pass to certified consistent density, certifiable result. One day. Premium rate.
Superior result. Total professional cost for the complete house at premium rates for short
targeted engagements: approximately €4,000 to €6,000. In a conventional build the
equivalent cost is €50,000 to €80,000 for prolonged engagements with whoever was available.

The minimum viable product of safety in this system is defined precisely: a structural
engineer for the load calculations and certification stamp, a licensed electrician for the
distribution board connections and DGEG declaration, a licensed plumber for the mains
connection, pressure testing, and ANQIP certification. Three professionals. Everything else

5.6 Why Off-Grid

When this system specifies an off-grid energy package as standard, the recommendation is not
ideological. It is practical, financial, and psychological — three separate arguments that
point in the same direction.

The financial argument is straightforward. Across Europe, energy companies have more than
doubled their net earnings over the past fifteen years while household energy costs have
tripled. The customer on the grid is not a customer in any meaningful sense — they are a
captive revenue stream with no exit. A solar system with battery storage in the Algarve
produces approximately 6,000 to 7,000 kWh annually from a 4kWp array. The sun does not send
a bill.

The resilience argument is equally direct. The grid fails. Not often, but when it does the
consequences for a grid-dependent household are immediate and serious — food spoils, heating
stops, cooking becomes impossible, light disappears. A nationwide grid event, a regional
infrastructure failure, a prolonged outage from storm damage: these are not hypothetical
scenarios. They happen. The off-grid house notices nothing. The same event that sends a
grid-dependent family to a hotel leaves the pallet RCP house family cooking dinner in a lit
kitchen with a full refrigerator.

The psychological argument is harder to quantify but may be the most durable of the three.
The permanent background anxiety of energy dependency — the monthly bill, the tariff
increases, the fear of an outage — simply disappears. It is not replaced by something else.
It is replaced by nothing, which is a different quality of daily life. Lower baseline stress.
A calmer relationship with the house and with money. The knowledge that whatever happens to
the infrastructure around you, your household is not affected.

No energy company will ever sell you that peace of mind. Selling it would mean losing you as
a customer forever.

Municipal water connection, where available and affordable, remains the default water
recommendation for this system — it is simpler, cheaper to connect, and requires no storage
management. Where municipal connection is not available or not practical, the water strategy
is site-specific: rainwater harvesting as a serious supplement in winter months, a borehole
as a long-term infrastructure investment, or stream-fed supply where legally permitted and
filtered to drinking standard. Water is too important to leave unsolved — and too
site-specific for a single universal answer.

5.7 The Structural Frame

Primary vertical and lateral loads are carried by the OSB skin diaphragm acting as a structural
box. The system does not require 2x6 studs at panel joints unless the reviewing engineer
specifically requires them for the certification submission. If specified, a 38x90mm softwood
stud at every 1250mm panel joint, running full wall height, carries primary loads and presents
to the engineer as a conventional stud frame with SIP panel infill.

The load path is explicit: vertical loads to the sill plate, lateral and racking loads to the
OSB diaphragm, floor and roof loads to the I-joists via manufacturer-published span tables,
pallet stringers as secondary distributed compression. The system does not depend on any single
pallet being perfect.

5.8 The Double Top Plate

Two 38x90mm sections screwed face-to-face create a composite top plate of 76 to 90mm depth.
Three stacked pallets at 800mm each produce 2,400mm. Addition of the 90mm plate brings total
height to 2,490mm — within 10mm of the OSB sheet height. The double top plate provides a wider
bearing surface for I-joist hangers and creates a clear structural load path from roof to wall
to foundation.

5.9 The T-Stack Wall — 3750mm Height

Where wall heights greater than 2,500mm are required, a full vertical panel of 2,500mm is
erected in the standard manner. A second full panel is placed horizontally on top, its 1,250mm
dimension providing additional height. A ledger plate carries the horizontal panel. The 2x6
structural stud runs continuously through both sections from sill to double top plate. Total wall
height: 3,750mm. No cutting required.

5.10 The Tongue-and-Groove Panel Interlock

The outer OSB skin extends 150mm beyond the pallet core on one side of each panel, creating a
tongue. The adjacent panel has its outer skin set back 150mm, creating a groove. When panels
are erected, the tongue slides into the groove, producing a mechanical interlock that prevents
lateral separation under wind load, eliminates air infiltration at joints, and provides
self-alignment during erection.

5.11 Construction Sequence

Day 1: Foundation prepared. Earth screws installed at load points. A treated timber sill plate
— 100 x 50mm — fixed around the full perimeter on top of the earth screws, creating a level
reference line for wall panel erection. Floor structure: 38x140mm (2x6) solid C16 softwood
joists at 400mm centres, bearing directly on the earth screws and sill plate. One-time
lightweight pallets laid flat between joists — support for glass wool insulation below floor
deck. Glass wool 140mm between joists. P5 T&G moisture resistant chipboard 22mm screwed to
joist tops — complete insulated floor deck. The 2x6 floor joist system is simpler, cheaper,
and more forgiving than I-joists for the floor — no engineering submission required for
informal builds, widely understood by any structural engineer for permitted builds.

Day 2: Wall panel manufacturing per protocol in section 5.2.5. All panels for the house
fabricated and stacked in sequence using the jig. Small inaccessible cavities — above and
below window openings, above door openings — pre-filled by hand with mineral wool before
panels are stacked. These zones cannot be reached by the blow-in insulation contractor
after assembly. One or two rolls of mineral wool handles all pre-fill zones. Panels cure
overnight under stack weight and ratchet straps.

Day 3: Wall panel erection. Structure only — no insulation yet. Erection begins from the
centre of each wall and builds outward. Tongue-and-groove interlock engaged at each panel
joint. Simpson Strong-Tie brackets at all connections. Double top plate fixed. The wet
wall — pre-assembled by plumber either off-site or on-site before this day — is installed
as a complete unit. Connect main water supply and drainage. Plumbing done.

Day 4: Ridge plate with all I-joist hangers pre-rigged on the ground at exact 820mm
spacing, lifted and fixed to top of wall panels. I-joists slotted into pre-rigged hangers
from A-frame step ladder, six screws each, immediately secure. One-time pallets —
pre-painted on ground — dropped into I-joist bays. Glass wool 50-80mm laid on top of
pallets. Steel sheet with pre-bonded condensation mat screwed to top flanges. Roof
complete and weathertight.

Day 5: Electrical cables pulled through open wall cavities by owner using EMT steel
conduit. All back boxes, conduit runs, and distribution board mounting completed. Windows
installed in prepared openings simultaneously.

Day 6: Inner OSB skins applied to wall panels. Gypsum board fixed immediately after.
Window reveals fitted from OSB and gypsum cutouts. Structure fully enclosed.

Day 7: Blow-in rockwool insulation contractor arrives. One person, one day. All large
wall cavities, roof I-joist bays, and floor cavities filled with blow-in loose rockwool
through injection points. Each cavity requires one injection point and one air escape
path. Consistent density throughout. Insulation complete in one day.
Earth screw installation: if not already done before Day 1, the earth screw contractor
installs the full foundation in one day using a compact hydraulic machine.

  Earth screws + blow-in rockwool insulation:   approximately €5,000
  Licensed electrician sign-off:                approximately €1,000
  Licensed plumber sign-off:                    approximately €1,000

6.4 The Failure Hierarchy — What Actually Fails First

Under extreme vertical compression the RCP panel in tall orientation does not fail by
buckling. The deck boards span approximately 300mm between stringer supports — a
slenderness ratio so low that the Euler buckling load is academic. The board would crush
in compression across its full 22mm thickness long before it could buckle. For practical
purposes, the pallet deck boards in this orientation are buckling-proof.

The 3x7 stud comparison in Chapter 10 is valid for cross-sectional timber area only and
should be understood in that context. The panel does not behave like a floor-to-ceiling
stud wall because the deck boards are not floor-to-ceiling columns. Each deck board spans
approximately 300mm between lateral supports — the stringer. A conventional 3x7 stud
spans the full wall height unsupported except at top and bottom. The deck board in the
RCP has approximately 70 times the buckling resistance of the equivalent solid stud
spanning 2500mm. This is a genuine structural advantage that the cross-section comparison
does not capture.

The honest failure hierarchy under extreme vertical load — most to least likely:

ONE — OSB SKIN NAIL HEAD PULL-THROUGH:
The most likely first failure mode. Under extreme vertical load the OSB skin attempts to
separate from the pallet surface. The nail heads bear against the OSB face and eventually
pull through the sheet. OSB has relatively low nail head pull-through resistance compared
to solid timber or plywood — the strands part around the nail head. This is the weakest
link in the composite system.

Mitigation: thicker OSB (18mm increases pull-through resistance approximately 20 percent),
closer nail spacing, structural screws with large bearing heads, connector plates at panel
edges.

TWO — ADHESIVE BOND SHEAR:
The Sikaflex 512 bond between OSB skin and deck board surface transfers shear across
the full bonding area. Published tensile strength approximately 2.5 N/mm² — very strong.
But progressive peel failure can initiate at panel edges if bond quality is inconsistent.
The peel test protocol in Appendix H exists specifically to catch inadequate bonds before
panels enter service.

THREE — PALLET DECK BOARD CRUSHING:
Softwood in compression parallel to grain at approximately 17 N/mm² across 64,680mm²
gives approximately 1,100 kN capacity for the timber alone. Single storey design demand
is approximately 1.74 kN. The timber crushing threshold is many times the actual demand.
Not a realistic failure mode for any application this system addresses.

FOUR — DECK BOARD BUCKLING:
Essentially impossible at 300mm unsupported length and 22mm thickness. Included for
completeness only.

THE IMPORTANT IMPLICATION:

The OSB skin is the weakest link — and the OSB is the most easily upgraded component.
Thicker OSB, more nails, structural screws — all increase connection transfer capacity
without changing any other element of the system. The pallet timber, the adhesive, and
the composite action all have substantially more capacity than the connection detail at
the OSB face. The system is limited by its most accessible and cheapest component, which
means the upgrade path is clear, costed, and predictable.

Additionally: the OSB skin is continuous across the full panel face. If one nail starts
to pull through, the skin redistributes load to adjacent nails automatically — because
it is a continuous structural diaphragm. Progressive failure is inherently resisted. A
conventional stud wall does not have this property — if one stud fails, adjacent studs
receive additional load without warning. The RCP composite action provides built-in
load redistribution at no additional cost.

7. THE FOUNDATION SYSTEM
================================================================================

7.1 The Default Recommendation — Earth Screws

Helical ground anchors — earth screws — are the default foundation recommendation for this
system. They are installed in one day by one operator with a compact hydraulic machine. No
excavation. No concrete. No formwork. No curing time. Load capacity of 15 to 25 kN per screw —
more than adequate for single storey timber frame construction under Lagos wind and load
conditions per Chapter 6 load cases. Fully removable and reusable. Engineer-certifiable from
manufacturer published load data and installation specifications. Cost approximately €2,000 to
€3,000 for a family home. Available from specialist suppliers throughout Portugal.

WHY NOT CONCRETE — THE ALGARVE FOUNDATION PROBLEM:

In Portugal and Spain, concrete foundations and slabs are the default. They are also
frequently executed poorly — because foundations are invisible, buried before anyone
can inspect them, and are the first place a contractor recovers margin. The mistakes
are buried literally and figuratively. They do not show up as cracks in the walls until
years later — sometimes a decade — as the Algarve's sandy clay soils expand and
contract with seasonal moisture cycles, erode under repeated heavy rain, and shift
under the rigid concrete that fights rather than accommodates the movement.

By the time the cracks appear, the contractor is long gone. The warranty has expired.
The remediation — underpinning a cracked concrete slab in shifting Algarve soil — is
among the most expensive construction operations available. A job that was cheap to
do correctly costs tens of thousands to fix after the fact. And it was always invisible,
so the corner-cutting was always undetectable.

The earth screw foundation bypasses the problematic topsoil entirely, reaching
consolidated subsoil through helical bearing plates. Each screw is independent — if
the soil shifts under one screw, the others continue to bear load. The rigid sill beam
bridges any irregularity across the full perimeter. The structure can redistribute
load across its entire perimeter, bridging foundation movements that would crack a
concrete slab catastrophically.

Earth screws satisfy structural requirements in most Portuguese municipalities for single storey
timber frame construction and can be submitted as part of a formal engineer stamp package
without a concrete slab. For a fully permitted build that is still demountable, earth screws are
the correct specification.

MONO-PITCH ROOF SLOPE — BUILT INTO THE FOUNDATION:

The drainage slope for the mono-pitch shed roof is achieved entirely through the earth
screw setting heights on the front long wall versus the back long wall. The earth screw
bracket heads on the front wall are set 300mm higher than those on the back wall.
Standard adjustable bracket heads accommodate this without any modification.

The floor joists span horizontally between the two sill beams regardless of their height
difference — the floor is perfectly level. The interior ceiling is perfectly flat. The
300mm height difference is invisible from inside the house. It exists only at wall plate
level and above.

Above the I-joist top flanges, timber firrings create the visible slope for the steel
sheet roof. The steel sheet runs from the high front wall plate down to the lower back
wall plate and drains to the rear.

Roof slope: 300mm rise over 7,000mm span = 4.3 percent — functional drainage in all
conditions. The foundation team sets the bracket heights during installation. One
measurement. No further adjustment required.

7.2 The Foundation Spectrum

REMOVABLE STONE OR CONCRETE PADS: Flat pads or precast pavers on levelled ground supporting
the treated timber sill plate. Zero cost. Fully demountable. No trace on removal. No engineer
submission possible. Appropriate for Positions 1 and 2 on the legal spectrum.

EARTH SCREWS — DEFAULT FOR ALL BUILDS: As described above. Appropriate for all five
positions on the legal spectrum. Always earth screws unless site conditions specifically
prevent it. The default foundation for every build — informal or fully permitted.

PAD FOOTINGS: Small concrete pads at structural load points only where bedrock or site
conditions prevent earth screw installation. No continuous slab. Cost approximately €1,500
to €2,500. A secondary option only — not a default recommendation.

7.3 The Basement Option — The Concrete Oreo

If a basement is required, the standard approach is to pour a concrete foundation — expensive,
permanent, heavy, and irreversible. This system offers a different approach. The same RCP
panel, fabricated with a different fill material, becomes a basement wall that is strong,
waterproof, fire resistant, acoustically excellent, and still demountable.

THE AIRCRETE-FILLED RCP:

Aircrete — foamed Portland cement — is a lightweight, insulating, fireproof material made by
infusing stable foam into a cement slurry. At 400 to 600 kg/m³ density it is roughly one third
to one quarter the weight of conventional concrete, fireproof, insect-proof, and moisture
resistant. It can be mixed with basic tools: Portland cement, dish detergent, xanthan gum
from any supermarket, and a drill with mixing paddle. The xanthan gum stabilises the soap
bubbles so they survive the wet cement mix.

The RCP panel is fabricated lying flat — outer marine plywood skin down, pallet core placed
on top, edges sealed. The open cavity is then filled with aircrete poured from above. The
pallet timber acts as a distributed rebar network through the cross section — providing
tensile reinforcement that aircrete alone lacks. After partial cure the inner marine plywood
skin goes on, nailed and glued as normal. The result is a panel that looks identical to a
standard RCP from the outside but has a concrete core that is fireproof, structurally massive,
and rot-proof indefinitely.

This is the Concrete Oreo. Marine plywood outside. Aircrete and pallet timber inside. Marine
plywood inside. The concrete is never seen. The panel connects to adjacent panels in timber —
exactly the same connection detail as the standard RCP. It looks like a wooden house. It
connects like a wooden house. It builds like a wooden house. Inside it is concrete.

FABRICATION SEQUENCE FOR BASEMENT PANELS:

1. Lay outer marine plywood skin on jig — waterproof face down
2. Place pallet core — pallets in standard orientation
3. Seal panel edges with timber blocking to contain the pour
4. Mix aircrete in batches — Portland cement, foam agent, water
5. Pour aircrete into cavity — fills all voids around pallet stringers and blocks
6. Allow 24 hour partial cure
7. Install lifting eyes — M12 or M16 lifting eye bolts threaded into stringer timber
8. Close with inner marine plywood skin — nails and Sikaflex as standard
9. Full cure — 48 to 72 hours before handling

LIFTING RINGS AND REVERSIBILITY:

Before closing the panel, install M12 or M16 lifting eye bolts into the top stringer at
two points per panel. The stringer is 75mm solid softwood — sufficient embedment depth for
a rated lifting eye at the panel's filled weight of approximately 200 to 240 kg per panel.
The rings fold flat when not in use. They protrude approximately 50mm above the panel top
edge — invisible once the above-grade wall panels are installed.

To remove a basement panel: attach lifting chains or straps to the rings, lift vertically
with a compact crane, chain hoist, or telehandler. The panel comes out of the ground cleanly.
Every component is intact and reusable. The basement disassembles.

BELOW-GRADE INSTALLATION:

Dig the perimeter trench to basement depth plus 300mm. Set aircrete RCP panels upright in
the trench — temporary timber bracing holds panels plumb during backfill. The top 300mm of
each panel extends above ground surface. The above-ground portion connects to the standard
RCP wall panels at the sill plate junction using the same timber connection detail. The
concrete inside the panel is never involved in the above-grade connection. The junction is
wood to wood — identical in detail to any other panel joint in the system.

Backfill against the marine plywood outer face — add a bituminous waterproof membrane
over the marine plywood before backfilling for full waterproofing. Standard practice for
any below-grade timber structure. Cost: approximately €5 to €8 per square metre of
membrane.

THE CONNECTION TO THE ABOVE-GRADE STRUCTURE:

The basement panel protrudes 300mm above grade. The sill plate of the above-grade RCP
wall sits on top of this 300mm stub — exactly as it would sit on an earth screw foundation
beam. The structural connection is timber to timber. The transition from concrete Oreo
below grade to standard rockwool RCP above grade is invisible in the finished building.

7.4 Marine Plywood — The Wet Environment Skin

Standard OSB3 is certified for use in humid conditions but is not appropriate as a
permanent below-grade or marine environment skin. For applications involving sustained
ground contact, groundwater, coastal salt air, or high humidity, the outer OSB skin is
replaced with marine plywood.

Marine plywood is manufactured with fully waterproof phenolic adhesive throughout — no
delamination under sustained moisture exposure. It is the strongest laminated timber
panel available. At 21 to 22mm thickness it is stiffer than 15mm OSB and provides
superior nail head pull-through resistance.

PRICING:
New marine plywood 22mm:           approximately €20 per half panel (1250 x 1250mm)
Second grade or reclaimed:         approximately €8 to €12 per half panel

Buying second grade or reclaimed marine plywood is entirely appropriate when the outer
face will be covered with epoxy or bituminous membrane — the surface quality is irrelevant
once coated. The structural and waterproof properties of the ply are not affected by
cosmetic imperfections. At second grade pricing the cost premium over OSB for the outer
skin is minimal.

APPLICATIONS FOR MARINE PLYWOOD SKIN:

Below-grade basement walls — replace outer OSB with marine plywood, add bituminous
membrane over the ply before backfilling. The panel is now functionally equivalent to
a concrete foundation wall at a fraction of the cost and with full reversibility.

Coastal and marine environments — replace outer OSB with marine plywood, coat with
exterior epoxy. Salt spray, driving rain, sustained humidity — the panel handles it
indefinitely. No repainting required. No swelling. No rot.

Saunas, pool houses, wet rooms, structures adjacent to water bodies — any application
where sustained surface moisture is expected on the exterior face.

High humidity agricultural buildings — livestock shelters, mushroom growing houses,
anywhere where condensation or cleaning water regularly contacts the wall surface.

The inner skin remains standard OSB3 or gypsum in all cases — the marine plywood
upgrade applies only to the face exposed to moisture. The fabrication protocol,
connection details, and nail specification are identical to the standard RCP.

8. THE ROOF SYSTEM
================================================================================

8.1 The I-Joist Roof — Geometry and Pallet Integration

I-joists are spaced at 820mm centres. This spacing is determined by the Euro pallet width of
800mm: the clear span between I-joist webs of approximately 800mm allows a one-time use
lightweight pallet to be dropped into the I-joist cavity from above, where it rests on the
bottom flange and is held in position laterally by the webs on each side. Gravity holds it in
place.

The pallets used for the roof ceiling are one-time use lightweight export pallets — not EPAL
certified units. Their structural requirement is minimal: rest in the I-joist bay without
falling, support the weight of glass wool above them, and look acceptable from below when
painted or whitewashed. They are available for free or near-free from logistics depots,
supermarkets, and garden centres. Significantly lighter than EPAL pallets — 8 to 12 kg versus
25 to 35 kg — making them easy to handle overhead.

Before installation, every one-time pallet is laid flat on the ground and whitewashed or
treated with boiled linseed oil. They are completely finished before they become a ceiling.
No overhead painting. No scaffolding. No second visits. The ceiling is complete the moment
the roof goes up.

Screws are driven through the I-joist web into the pallet deck boards — three screws per bay,
one at each pallet stringer position. This transfers the pallet weight through the web in shear
— the force path that I-joists are designed to carry — rather than creating tensile pull on the
web-to-bottom-flange glue joint. As an additional structural benefit, the screwed pallets act
as continuous lateral restraint to the I-joist compression flange, preventing lateral torsional
buckling across the full roof span. The composite assembly is structurally superior to I-joists
alone.

8.2 The Insulation and Ventilation Strategy

Above the one-time pallets, a 50 to 80mm layer of glass wool — lã mineral de vidro, available
at Leroy Merlin Portugal at approximately 3.83 to 4.29 euros per square metre — is laid on the
pallet surface. This glass wool layer holds still air in the ventilation gap above it.

A ventilation gap of 4 to 5 centimetres is maintained between the top surface of the glass wool
and the underside of the steel roof sheet. This gap is sealed at the gable ends — not open to
wind circulation. Still air is one of the most effective insulators available. The one-time
pallet seals the cavity from below. The glass wool holds the still air above it. The steel sheet
closes the cavity from above.

8.3 The Steel Roof — With Pre-Bonded Condensation Mat

Steel corrugated or trapezoidal roof sheet is screwed directly to the I-joist top flanges. The
sheet spans 820mm between top flanges — within the spanning capacity of standard steel roof
profiles. The steel sheet specified for this system includes a pre-bonded condensation mat on
its underside.

A separate condensation mat installed at height before the steel sheet goes down is a significant
risk: it can tear, shift, or misalign during installation. The factory-bonded mat eliminates all
of these risks. It is perfectly aligned with the steel surface and requires no additional site
operations. The additional cost over plain steel sheet is justified by the elimination of a
failure-prone installation step.

8.4 The Pre-Rigged Ridge Plate — No Scaffolding Required

All I-joist hangers are screwed to the ridge plate on the ground before any lifting begins. The
plate arrives on the wall top with every hanger already positioned at exact 820mm spacing,
already oriented correctly, already fixed. Two people lift it and screw it to the double top plate.

Each I-joist is then lifted to the pre-rigged hanger, slotted in, and secured with six screws —
three from each side. From an A-frame step ladder. The I-joist is immediately stable. It cannot
fall. The person on the ladder does one thing: guide the joist into the slot and drive six
screws. No scaffolding. No stillage. One A-frame step ladder. The entire roof structure is
erected from the floor.

8.5 The Ceiling

From below, the completed roof assembly presents the exposed bottom flanges of the I-joists at
820mm rhythm, and between them the painted or whitewashed underside of the one-time pallets.
The joints between pallet boards are invisible — the I-joist flanges frame each bay as a

9. THE BUILDING SITE
================================================================================

A conventional construction site is defined by its chaos. Concrete dust. Sawdust. Paint spills.
Offcuts piled in corners. Skips overflowing. Noise from angle grinders, circular saws,
generators, compressors, concrete mixers. The site deteriorates for weeks before it begins to
improve.

This building site is different from day one.

There is no concrete mixer. No diesel generator. No compressor. No saw. No sawdust. No paint
spills — all painting happens on flat surfaces on the ground, controlled and contained. No
offcuts — because nothing is cut. No skip — because there is almost no waste.

The site runs on two battery packs charged the night before. From day four, the house's own
solar panel charges the batteries. The building site becomes self-powered before it is finished.

The site is quiet. A battery drill. A nail gun if you choose. The sound of people talking,
making decisions, working together. Children painting pallet boards on the ground. The smell of
pine tar wood treatment warming in the sun.

Six hours of productive work per day is enough. Tired people make mistakes. Six focused hours
of parallel work, planned in advance, produces more than twelve hours of conventional
construction.

A conventional build of 80m² generates approximately 8 to 12 tonnes of construction waste.
This build generates: OSB and I-joist packaging — cardboard, recyclable. Empty Sikaflex tubes.
Empty glass wool packaging. Zero structural offcuts, because nothing is cut. Total waste: fits
in the back of a car.

This building site would not disturb a neighbour. It would not require a safety officer. It
could operate in a residential street without complaint. It is a site that children can safely
be part of — passing materials, painting boards, filling joints — contributing meaningfully to
a building they will remember making for the rest of their lives.

That is not a side effect of the system. It is a consequence of the design philosophy applied
consistently from the first component to the last.

10. STANDARDISATION AS DESIGN PHILOSOPHY
================================================================================

10.1 The Five Enemies

This system is defined as much by what it eliminates as by what it builds. Five enemies
have been identified and systematically removed from the build process.

ENEMY ONE — COMPLEXITY: Master-level carpentry knowledge. Improvisation on site.
Judgment calls that require years of experience. Measuring and cutting to non-standard
dimensions. Any decision that cannot be made by following the protocol. Eliminated by
the jig, the standardised dimensions, and the IKEA-Tesla design philosophy: every step
follows inevitably from the previous one, every connection fits in one orientation only,
every panel is identical to the last.

The critical insight: in conventional stud frame construction, squareness is the
builder's responsibility. Every stud must be checked, every corner must be verified,
every wall must be kept plumb during assembly. This requires skill developed over years.
In this system, squareness is built into the components. The OSB sheet is factory-cut
to exact dimensions. The EPAL pallet is certified to global dimensional tolerances.
The jig forces every panel to be identical. As long as the panel is assembled
horizontally and the jig is square, the panel is square. No skill required for
squareness — it is an engineered property of the assembly.

Even the blow-in rockwool insulation is an outsourced complexity. The contractor arrives
with a machine, fills the wall cavities in one pass to consistent density, and leaves.
The builder does not need to know how to cut and fit mineral wool batts, how to avoid
compression or gaps, or how to achieve consistent thermal performance. Outsource the
complexity. Pay the contractor. Get certified performance without developing the skill.

ENEMY TWO — SCAFFOLDING AND LADDERS: Everything that requires working at height.
Scaffolding costs money to hire, takes one to two days to erect, takes another day to
disassemble, creates overhead work, and is responsible for the majority of construction
site fall injuries. The design benchmark for an experienced RCP team: complete the
structural shell before a conventional crew has finished erecting and dismantling the
scaffolding for an equivalent conventional build. Scaffolding assembly and disassembly
for a standard house takes approximately four days total. The RCP shell for an
experienced team takes the same or less.

The flat fabrication principle eliminates scaffolding entirely. Every operation that
would normally require working at height — cladding the upper wall, painting above
head height, fitting the ceiling — is done on the ground before the wall is ever
raised. Paint applied horizontally does not drip and does not run. The result is
dramatically better than paint applied vertically at height, with essentially zero
skill required. The only time anyone briefly works above head height is during panel
erection and roof installation — both short operations.

ENEMY THREE — CUTTING AND MEASURING: The full sequence of measure, mark, cut is
eliminated wherever possible. Measuring introduces human error. Cutting is irreversible
— a board cut too short cannot be uncut. Compound errors accumulate across a build:
one millimetre off at the first panel becomes ten millimetres off at the tenth. This
system eliminates both simultaneously by designing around standard component dimensions.
OSB arrives at panel size. Cladding boards get one cut each to a fixed stop — no
measuring. Gypsum fits without cutting. I-joists ordered to length. Steel roof sheets
ordered to match the house span exactly. The jig ensures every panel is identical
without a single measurement on site.

ENEMY FOUR — CONCRETE AND DIGGING: You cannot have concrete without digging. A
concrete foundation requires excavation, formwork, rebar, pouring, and curing time.
Every step is irreversible once completed. In the Algarve specifically, concrete
foundations in sandy clay soils crack as the soil shifts seasonally. The mistakes are
invisible until years later and catastrophically expensive to remedy. Concrete is
responsible for approximately 8 percent of global CO₂ emissions.

Earth screws eliminate both simultaneously. No digging. The screw threads into
consolidated subsoil through the problematic topsoil layer, displacing and compacting
rather than removing material. One operator, one day, one machine. Fully reversible —
the screw reverses out and leaves no trace.

ENEMY FIVE — SEQUENTIAL WORKING: The municipal worker analogy. Five people standing
watching one person work. One trade waiting for the previous trade to finish before
they can begin. Linear construction where a delay in one task cascades into delays
in everything downstream. This system runs three parallel teams simultaneously from
day one — assembly, paint, foundation. Nobody waits. If a primary task is blocked,
a secondary task is always ready. The build is a mind map with fifty things that can
be done at any time.

10.2 The SpaceX Principle

The best part is no part. Elon Musk's first principle for hardware design at SpaceX:
if you can eliminate a component entirely, that is always better than optimising it.
Every component that does not exist cannot fail, cannot cost money, cannot require
maintenance. Only when elimination is impossible do you ask: what is the best possible
version of this component, and can it serve more than one function?

This principle runs through the entire RCP system.

The composting toilet eliminates the soil pipe, the cistern, the water supply to the
toilet, and the sewage connection — four components replaced by zero. The earth screws
eliminate the concrete slab, the formwork, the rebar, the mixer, and the curing time —
five components and weeks of waiting replaced by one. The mono-pitch roof eliminates
the ridge beam, the valley, the hip, the complex junction details — all replaced by a
single plane of steel sheet. The one wet wall eliminates distributed plumbing throughout
the house — all water connections concentrated in one location, one plumber, one visit.

When a component cannot be eliminated, it serves multiple functions. The EPAL pallet
is structural core, conduit highway, salt reservoir base, and thermal mass carrier
simultaneously. The OSB skin is structural sheathing, nailing surface for cladding,
substrate for gypsum, and panel erection handle simultaneously. The earth screws are
foundation, levelling system, and roof slope mechanism simultaneously.

The most reliable system is the one with the fewest parts. The most maintainable system
is the one where every remaining part serves multiple functions. This is not minimalism
for aesthetic reasons. It is engineering discipline applied to residential construction
— a field that has historically added components rather than eliminated them because
complexity justifies fees.

10.3 What if IKEA Made a House?

What if IKEA made a house? The question sounds absurd until you examine what IKEA
actually achieved — a system so well designed that ordinary people with no woodworking
skills reliably assemble complex, functional, attractive furniture from a wordless
instruction sheet. The secret was never the customer. It was the design. Every component
has a defined place. Every joint has one correct orientation. Every step follows
inevitably from the previous one. When the design is good enough, assembly requires no
skill — only patience and the ability to follow a sequence.

Tesla applied the same philosophy to manufacturing. Every component is designed so that
it can only be installed one way. The connector only fits in one direction. The bracket
only aligns in one position. The wrong orientation is physically impossible, not just
discouraged. If it goes in, it is correct. If it does not go in, something is wrong.
Binary. Unambiguous. No interpretation required.

The RCP system applies these same principles to residential construction. The jig
replaces skill. The single-size OSB sheet covering one panel face with zero cuts refuses
to let you make a cutting error. The standardised socket hole at 300mm x 300mm bottom
right corner of every panel is pre-drilled — no measuring on site, no decisions, no
mistakes. Every decision point is either completely unambiguous and completely
skill-independent, or it gets redesigned until it is.

Conventional construction is not designed as a system. It is an accumulation of historical
practices that nobody has had the incentive to question, layered over decades until the
overhead cost exceeds the material cost. The architect designs the building. The structural
engineer calculates the structure. The plumber designs the wet services. The electrician
designs the electrical system. Each discipline optimises for their own scope. Nobody
optimises for the whole.

The 70 percent overhead in conventional construction is primarily labour cost — skilled
professionals earning professional day rates for every hour on site. That labour cost is
not padding or fraud. It is the genuine market cost of skilled people. The RCP system
does not eliminate the need for skilled people — it concentrates them. One electrician.
One plumber. One insulation contractor. One earth screw operator. Four professionals
for four days rather than fifteen trades across fifteen months. Everything else is
assembly that any motivated person can do. The skill cost collapses. The result does not.

The result is a building that works eventually, after delays, overruns, and rework. But
it has never been designed as a system. It has been assembled by a sequence of specialists
none of whom were asked to consider the full picture.

The RCP system was designed from first principles as a system. Every element exists to
eliminate something. The jig eliminates skill dependency from panel fabrication. The earth
screws eliminate concrete, excavation, and curing time. The one wet wall eliminates the
complexity of distributed plumbing. The blow-in rockwool contractor eliminates the
inconsistency of hand-packing insulation. The double top plate eliminates the need for
precision levelling of the foundation. The pre-assembled wet wall eliminates wet trades
during assembly. The EMT conduit eliminates the fire criticism.

Each elimination removes not just the thing itself but everything that thing interacted
with — the delays it caused, the skills it required, the failures it enabled, the costs
it generated.

Complexity is not sophistication. Complexity is the accumulated cost of not thinking hard
enough about the problem. The conventional house build has never been redesigned from
first principles because the people who build it make more money when it is complex.
Complexity justifies fees. Complexity creates dependency. Complexity is the product the
conventional system sells.

The effort in this system goes into the design, not the execution. Design it right once.
Execute it simply many times. The house that cannot be built wrong is better than the
house that requires expert execution at every step.

10.3 The Principle of Standardisation

This system works because every dimension is derived from standard material dimensions.
The OSB sheet is 2500 x 1250mm. The EPAL pallet is 1200 x 800 x 145mm. Three pallets
at 800mm produce 2400mm of wall height. The 90mm composite double top plate brings total
height to 2490mm — within 10mm of the OSB sheet height. The gypsum board is 1200mm wide,
the panel is 1250mm wide — the 50mm difference is covered by joint tape, zero cutting
required. Every dimension fits because the design was built around the materials.

This is not compromise. It is correct engineering. The complexity of conventional
construction comes from forcing non-standard dimensions into a site-specific design. Every
non-standard dimension requires a cut. Every cut requires a tool, time, waste, and skill.
Eliminating cuts eliminates all of those costs simultaneously.

10.4 The Stud Comparison — Full Composite Cross Section

The correct comparison for the RCP panel is not against 3x7 posts — a section nobody
uses. The correct comparison is against the 2x6 stud wall that is the standard for
residential timber frame construction across Europe and North America.

STEP ONE — PALLET TIMBER CROSS SECTION:

Per pallet in tall orientation:
  Top deck: 3 boards at 145x22mm + 2 boards at 100x22mm = 13,970mm²
  Bottom deck: 1 board at 145x22mm + 2 boards at 100x22mm = 7,590mm²
  Total per pallet: 21,560mm²

Three pallets: 3 x 21,560 = 64,680mm²

STEP TWO — OSB SKIN CONTRIBUTION:

Two OSB3 skins, each 15mm thick, panel width 1250mm:
  Per skin: 1250 x 15 = 18,750mm²
  Both skins: 18,750 x 2 = 37,500mm²

STEP THREE — TOTAL COMPOSITE CROSS SECTION:

Pallet timber: 64,680mm²
OSB both skins: 37,500mm²
TOTAL: 102,180mm²

STEP FOUR — EQUIVALENT IN 2x6 STUDS:

A standard 2x6 stud in metric: 38mm x 140mm = 5,320mm²

Number of equivalent 2x6 studs:
102,180 / 5,320 = 19.2 studs

STEP FIVE — WHAT SPACING DOES THAT REPRESENT?

Panel width 1250mm across 19.2 equivalent studs:
1250 / 19.2 = 65mm centres

RESULT: The composite cross section of one 1250mm RCP panel is equivalent to 2x6
studs placed at 65mm centres. Standard construction uses 400 to 600mm centres.
The RCP panel contains six to nine times the structural material of a conventional
2x6 stud wall of the same width.

Under single storey domestic loading the panel operates at well under 10 percent of
its theoretical compression capacity. The structural case is not marginal. It is
overwhelming.

The comparison also ends there. The pallet deck boards are not floor-to-ceiling
columns. Each deck board spans approximately 300mm between stringer supports —
dramatically shorter than a 2x6 stud spanning the full 2500mm wall height. Shorter
unsupported length means dramatically higher buckling resistance. The RCP panel is
not equivalent to a 2x6 stud wall at 65mm centres. It is structurally superior to
one.

10.5 Customisation — The Honest Trade-Off

You can customise this house. Any window size. Any door position. Any internal layout.
Any finish, any colour, any furniture. What you cannot change without cost is the wall
height, the panel width, or the panel depth — those dimensions are set by OSB, pallet,
and I-joist. Step outside those dimensions and you introduce cuts, waste, and time.

The philosophy in one sentence: let the materials create the constraints, and be as
creative as possible within them.

10.6 The Only Cuts in This System

Two cuts. That is the complete list.

The window opening: a jigsaw through the OSB outer skin. The door opening: same.

No circular saw. No mitre saw. No table saw. A jigsaw for window and door openings,
a handsaw for the occasional pallet deck board trim. Tools that cost under €50 combined.

10.4 The Zero-Cut Interior

Gypsum board at Leroy Merlin Portugal is 1200mm wide and available in 2500mm length. A wall
panel is 1250mm wide. The gypsum sheet covers the panel in one piece with 50mm overlap at the
joint — covered by joint tape and filler. Zero cutting for width. Zero cutting for height. Every
sheet goes up whole. For an 80m² house with approximately 200m² of interior wall surface, that
is 40 sheets installed in two to three hours with no measuring, no scoring, and no fitting.

No saw means no blade. No blade means no cutting injury. No sawdust means no respiratory hazard.
No noise means no hearing protection required. The site is safe enough for children to be
present and quiet enough to work without disturbing neighbours.

11. THE WINDOW AND DOOR SYSTEM
================================================================================

11.1 Window Size — Any Size is Possible

The system imposes two fixed constraints on windows and nothing else:

Sill height: always 800mm from floor — the height of one pallet. This is ergonomically
appropriate for a living space and is a direct consequence of the pallet geometry.

Minimum width: 800mm — one pallet width. Any opening narrower than this would cut into the
structural stringer assembly and is not recommended without engineer review.

Above those two constraints, any window size is achievable. A 2400mm wide floor-to-ceiling
opening is possible by removing pallets across three adjacent panels. A narrow 800mm slit
window is possible by removing one pallet from one panel. The width steps in 800mm increments
— one, two, or three pallets across — and the height is continuously adjustable by cutting the
top pallet deck boards at any point.

11.2 The Standard Opening — Starting Point

A standard window opening is created by removing the middle pallet from a three-pallet panel.
The resulting opening is 1200mm wide and 800mm tall — the footprint of one EPAL pallet. The
window sill sits at 800mm from the floor — the height of one pallet.

11.3 Adjusting the Window Height

For taller windows, the top pallet is partially cut. The cut goes through the deck boards only,
parallel to the ground, between the load-bearing blocks. The stringer assembly is not touched.
A jigsaw or handsaw makes this cut in thirty seconds.

The depth of the cut determines the window height:
  Cut 200mm from top pallet: window height 1000mm, top of window at 1800mm
  Cut 300mm from top pallet: window height 1100mm, top of window at 1900mm
  Cut 400mm from top pallet: window height 1200mm, top of window at 2000mm

The cut portion of the top pallet goes to the firewood pile — the only accepted waste in the
window system. The remaining upper portion of the top pallet stays in the wall, still glued and
screwed to the OSB skin, still contributing to panel stiffness.

11.4 The H Frame — Structural Reinforcement

Removing a pallet from a panel eliminates the diaphragm contribution of the OSB skin across
that zone. Two full-height 2x6 studs — one on each side of the window opening, immediately
beside the opening — restore the structural integrity of the section. These studs run the full
wall height from sill to double top plate, carrying vertical and lateral loads around the opening.

Above and below the window opening, OSB strips cut from the window cutout are screwed flat
against the pallet faces, spanning between the two vertical studs. These strips close the
opening at top and bottom, give the window frame something solid to fix to, and cost nothing —
they are the material removed from the opening itself. No separate window frame timber needs to
be purchased. The window drops into the H and is fixed to the 2x6 studs on each side and the
OSB strips above and below.

11.5 Windows and Doors — A Significant Budget Decision

Windows and doors are the most significant variable in the finishing budget after the off-grid
package. The opening is free — it is made from material already in the system. What goes in the
opening is a choice with a wide cost range and meaningful performance consequences.

SECOND-HAND WINDOWS: The cheapest option and entirely legitimate for informal builds and
secondary structures. Architectural salvage yards, online marketplaces, and demolition sites
in Portugal regularly yield double-glazed PVC or aluminium frames in good condition for €20
to €80 per unit. The opening in this system is adjustable — if a second-hand window is a
non-standard size, the top pallet cut and H frame adapt to suit it. The window determines
the opening, not the other way around. For a guest house, a garden room, or a sauna, second-
hand windows are the correct specification.

STANDARD PVC DOUBLE GLAZED: Available at Leroy Merlin Portugal from approximately €80 to
€150 per unit in standard sizes. Adequate thermal performance for the Algarve climate. The
baseline specification for a permanent home.

TRIPLE GLAZED: Approximately €200 to €400 per unit. Meaningful energy saving in a climate
where heating is required more than a few weeks per year. In the Algarve the payback period
for triple over double glazing is longer than in northern Europe — the mild winter reduces
the benefit. However for a client who wants the maximum thermal envelope performance and is
building for the long term, triple glazing is the correct specification. Combined with the
0.40 W/m2K wall U-value, triple glazed windows produce a building envelope approaching
Passive House standard without any additional structural complexity.

DOORS: The same spectrum applies. A solid hardwood reclaimed door costs €50 to €150 and
looks extraordinary. A standard new PVC door costs €200 to €400. A thermally broken aluminium
door with triple glazing costs €800 to €1,500. All fit the same opening. Choose based on
budget, aesthetic preference, and thermal ambition.

The window and door budget for an 80m² house with six windows and two doors:
  Second-hand: approximately €300 to €600 total
  Standard new double glazed: approximately €800 to €1,400 total
  Triple glazed throughout: approximately €1,800 to €3,500 total

This is one of the few budget lines in the system where spending more produces a measurably
better physical result. The wall is already excellent. The windows are where thermal bridges
occur and where the envelope can be strengthened most cost-effectively.

11.6 The Window Reveal — From the Cutout

The OSB and gypsum cutouts from the window opening are cut into strips and used to line the
window reveal — the exposed wall surface between the window frame and the interior and exterior
wall faces. This converts the waste from the window cut into the finish of the window itself.

OSB strips are cut slightly wider than gypsum strips. When the gypsum reveal strips are fitted,
the wall gypsum board overlaps and hides the edge of the gypsum reveal strip — a clean,
invisible joint. Two separate cuts are made for this reason — not one simultaneous cut — because
the dimensional difference between OSB and gypsum strip widths is required for the clean joint
detail.

The window sill — the horizontal surface at the bottom of the reveal — is made from the OSB
cutout, doubled in thickness for rigidity, and whitewashed or painted before installation.

11.7 The Door Opening

A door opening is created by omitting one or two panels from the wall assembly. The opening
width is one or two panel widths — 1250mm or 2500mm. Standard door frames fit within 1250mm. A
structural header spans across the top of the opening between the panels on each side. The door
frame is prefabricated on the ground and placed into the opening. No cutting required.

11.8 Parallel Installation — Windows and Electricity

While the electrician pulls cables through open wall cavities on Day 5, a second person
simultaneously installs windows in the prepared openings. A third person fits OSB reveal strips.
By the time the electrician is finished, all windows are in and all reveals are lined. The inner
OSB and gypsum go up on Day 6, and the gypsum reveal strips are fitted immediately after,
completing each window in one continuous operation with no return visits.

12. FIRE STRATEGY
================================================================================

12.1 The Real Fire Risk

The primary fire risk in any timber frame building is not the timber itself — it is continuous
air cavities that allow fire to spread unseen through the structure. This is called concealed
cavity fire spread and it is what building codes address most strictly.

12.2 The Insulation and Fire Strategy — Blow-In Rockwool

The insulation system serves two purposes simultaneously: thermal performance and fire
containment. Both are achieved by the same material — loose blow-in rockwool — applied
by a specialist contractor in one day after the structure is assembled and enclosed.

BLOW-IN ROCKWOOL — LARGE CAVITIES: All large wall panel cavities, roof I-joist bays,
and floor joist bays are filled with blow-in loose rockwool through injection points. Each
cavity requires one injection point and one air escape path. The contractor fills each
cavity to consistent density using a calibrated blower. One person, one day for the
complete house. Rockwool is non-combustible — it does not burn, does not contribute to
fire spread, and does not melt below approximately 1,000 degrees Celsius. Fully packed
cavities eliminate the continuous air path that allows concealed cavity fire spread. This
is a standard approved fire containment solution familiar to engineers and fire officers
throughout European residential construction.

HAND-PACKED MINERAL WOOL — SMALL CAVITIES: Small inaccessible zones that the blow-in
contractor cannot reach — above and below window openings, above door openings,
wall-roof junctions at double top plate, floor perimeter at sill line, gable end ventilation
gap seals — are pre-filled by hand with standard mineral wool before panels are stacked
or before the blow-in contractor arrives. One to two rolls of mineral wool handles all
pre-fill zones across the complete house. These zones are filled during panel fabrication
or early assembly, not by the insulation contractor.

The combined system ensures complete fill with no voids anywhere in the structure.

12.3 The Four-Layer Fire Strategy

This system addresses fire risk through four independent layers. Each layer was chosen
primarily for another purpose — thermal performance, electrical safety, pest resistance,
weather protection. The fire protection is a secondary benefit that comes at no
additional cost.

LAYER ONE — GYPSUM TYPE F ON ALL INTERIOR SURFACES:
15mm Type F fire-rated gypsum board provides 30 minutes fire resistance. Gypsum
contains approximately 21 percent crystalline water by weight. Under fire conditions
it releases this water as steam, cooling the structure behind it and dramatically
slowing fire spread. Standard approved fire containment solution throughout European
residential construction.

LAYER TWO — EMT STEEL CONDUIT FOR ALL ELECTRICAL CABLING:
All electrical cables run inside steel conduit. Steel does not burn. If an electrical
fault occurs inside the conduit, the fault is contained within the steel tube. The
cable cannot ignite the surrounding timber because there is no direct contact between
cable and timber anywhere in the system.

LAYER THREE — STEEL ROOF:
In southern Europe, the primary fire threat to buildings is not internal ignition but
external — flying embers from forest fires landing on roofs. Research on the 2017
Pedrógão Grande fire complex in Portugal found that over 60 percent of structures were
ignited by firebrand deposition, and over 60 percent of those ignitions occurred on
the roof (Rodrigues et al., 2020). The roof is the primary weak point.

A steel sheet roof does not ignite. Embers land on it and die. No ember penetrates
steel sheet. No surface fire establishes on steel sheet. In a region where forest
fires are an annual occurrence — the Algarve included — a steel roof eliminates the
primary external ignition pathway entirely. This is not a marginal improvement. It is
the single most important passive fire defence for a building in a fire-prone landscape.

LAYER FOUR — A SALTY PEST INSURANCE:
Rodents chewing through electrical cables inside walls is one of the most common causes
of house fires. The salt layer in the base of every wall panel and floor joist bay
eliminates rodent habitation in the structure. No rodents means no chewed cables. No
chewed cables means no rodent-caused electrical fires.

The house is not fireproof. No timber house is. But the four-layer system addresses
every realistic ignition pathway: external ember attack eliminated by the steel roof,
internal electrical faults contained by EMT conduit, fire spread slowed by gypsum
barriers, and rodent-caused ignition eliminated by salt. Combined with fully packed
rockwool cavities that eliminate concealed cavity fire spread, this is a comprehensive
fire strategy using materials already in the system for other reasons.

13. INTERIOR AND EXTERIOR FINISH

13.0 Rediscovering What Works

A core principle of this system: some of the best materials and methods in construction
fell out of use not because they were inferior, but because industrial capital found it
profitable to replace them with expensive modern alternatives. Vinyl paint replaced
limewash. Chemical timber treatments replaced pine tar. Laminate flooring replaced end
grain timber. Pesticide programmes replaced salt.

None of the replacements are better. Most are worse. Vinyl paint traps moisture, grows
mould under the surface, and needs repainting every few years. Chemical timber treatments
are toxic, require safety precautions, and need renewal. Laminate flooring scratches,
swells, and cannot be repaired — only replaced. Pesticide programmes kill the target
pests, damage surrounding biology, and need annual reapplication.

The ancient materials are better. And today we can improve them further. Modern mineral
pigments in limewash produce any colour — not just white, not just the limited palette
of traditional practice. Modern pine tar formulations can be tinted to warm tones —
not just Viking black but ochre, red-brown, grey-green. Modern understanding of oil
chemistry means linseed oil can be applied more effectively than craftsmen of a century
ago managed. Ancient material. Modern knowledge. Superior result.

The houses still standing from a hundred years ago — the ones built by people with no
capital, no power tools, and no engineered materials — were finished with these exact
materials. They are still standing. The synthetic replacements that followed them are
already degraded.

13.1 The Two Finishes

This building system uses two surface treatments. Both are ancient. Both are available
everywhere. Both outperform the synthetic alternatives that replaced them. Neither contains
plastic.

PINE TAR TREATMENT — for all exterior timber cladding and outdoor surfaces. Pine tar,
boiled linseed oil, and turpentine in equal parts, warmed before application. The mixture
penetrates rather than coats — it protects wood from within. Naturally preservative,
water-resistant, and deeply antibacterial. Weathers to a beautiful dark tone. Used on
Scandinavian boat timber and exterior structures for centuries. Apply flat on the ground
before cladding boards go onto the panels. Fire safety precautions required during
application — linseed oil and pine tar are flammable during application and while curing.

BOILED LINSEED OIL — for all interior exposed timber. I-joist flanges, ceiling pallet
boards, shelving, furniture built from the same materials as the house. Penetrates the
wood cell structure and polymerises in place. Produces a warm amber finish that deepens
with age. No strong smell once fully cured. Safe indoors. Multiple thin coats, each
absorbed fully before the next.

LIMEWASH — for walls, ceilings, and any surface intended to be bright. Slaked lime and
water. Tint with any natural pigment — ochre, raw umber, iron oxide, charcoal — for a
vast range of colour options. Used continuously for thousands of years across the
Mediterranean, the Middle East, and Scandinavia. Breathable. Naturally mould-resistant
— lime is alkaline and hostile to biological growth. Antibacterial. Does not peel.
New layers can be applied directly over old ones at any time. When applied with a wide
brush in cross patterns it creates an irregular cloud-like texture that is genuinely
beautiful — alive with movement as light changes through the day. Technique is learned
from a YouTube video in twenty minutes. Anyone can do it. A complete interior for an
84m² house costs under €300 in materials. First coats can be applied horizontally while
panels are flat — faster, no drips, better coverage. Final coat after erection for the
finished result.

Micro cement — also called micro concrete — is the currently fashionable wall finish
in high-end interiors and hotel lobbies. Applied in six to seven coats by a specialist
applicator. Cost: up to €250 per square metre installed. The result is a seamless,
matte, textured surface with depth and movement. A well-applied cross-pattern limewash
produces a finish that is visually indistinguishable from micro cement to any
non-specialist eye. For bathroom walls where abrasion and water resistance matter,
micro cement is correct. For every other wall surface in the house, limewash is the
correct specification. The ancient method. The dramatically cheaper result. Nobody can
tell the difference.

No plastic paint. No synthetic sealers. No petroleum-derived varnish. These are the
correct choices, arrived at by civilisations that had thousands of years to find them.
They happen to cost almost nothing.

13.2 The Interior — Standard Finish

After electrical installation, 15mm Type F fire-rated gypsum board — 2500mm x 1200mm
— is fixed to the inner OSB skin. The gypsum board is 50mm narrower than the OSB panel
width of 1250mm. This 50mm difference is handled with a filler strip at the panel edge
or a slightly wider joint compound application — no cutting required. The dimensions
are compatible by design. Limewash over primed gypsum, applied with a wide brush in
cross patterns for an irregular cloud texture. Any colour. Any technique. Completely
individual. From inside, the house is indistinguishable from a well-finished home that
cost far more to build.

13.3 The Interior — Decorative Board Alternative

The RCP panel is 2500mm tall and 1250mm wide. Standard sheet materials sold at every European
builders merchant are 2500 x 1250mm — the same dimension. This is not a coincidence. It is the
dimension of the OSB sheet that underlies the entire construction industry. The consequence for
interior finishing is significant: any 2500 x 1250mm decorative board covers exactly one panel
face with zero cuts. One joint between panels. The board arrives finished. Press it flat, fix
it, done.

Available options at Leroy Merlin and equivalent builders merchants, approximately €15 to €25
per board:

White melamine-faced chipboard:   clean, wipe-clean, modern. approximately €18 per board
Wood veneer-faced MDF:            oak, pine, walnut. warm, textured. approximately €22 per board
Birch plywood-faced:              natural, strong, screw anywhere. approximately €20 per board
Raw OSB3:                         industrial aesthetic. extremely cheap. approximately €12 per board

One joint between every panel — a 10mm shadow gap or a thin timber batten covers it cleanly.
No painting. No skim coat. No plaster. No trade. The board arrives with its finished face.

Cost for a complete 26-panel house interior at €20 average per board: approximately €520 in
materials. No plastering, no painting, no primer, no tools beyond an adhesive gun and a drill.

Gypsum Type F 15mm remains the correct specification for the ceiling and any surface where fire

Algarve climate even imperfect insulation is dramatically better than no insulation. In cold
climates where every void matters, the contractor is more worthwhile.

13.4 Low-Cost Luxury Details

The savings from the structural build become the budget for the finish. Spent correctly,
a modest finish budget produces results that look far more expensive than the outlay.

Furniture built from leftover OSB offcuts and surplus pallets — floating bed frames,
shelving, benches, storage units — with simple finishing techniques produces results that
look deliberately designed rather than improvised. The material is already in the system.
The effort is in the making. A floating bed with concealed lighting underneath, built
from the same OSB and pallets as the house itself, looks like a considered piece of
furniture. Because it is.

Black matte tap fittings cost no more than chrome equivalents and look dramatically
more considered. Rope door handles. Linen curtains. A single pendant light over the
dining table positioned correctly. Lavender at the entrance. These additions engage
smell and touch in ways that disproportionately affect the perceived quality of a space.
None of them are expensive. All of them are intentional.

Limewash is the correct wall finish for this system. It is extremely cheap, naturally
mould-resistant — lime is alkaline and hostile to biological growth — and endlessly
variable. Tint it with any natural pigment: ochre, raw umber, iron oxide, charcoal.
The colour range is vast. The finish changes completely depending on application
technique — smooth and even with a wide brush in long strokes, heavily textured and
layered with a circular motion, deliberately uneven for a worn patina. No two limewash
walls look the same. The wall is alive with movement as the light changes through the
day in a way that flat emulsion paint never achieves. Multiple thin coats build depth.
Imperfections in the gypsum below become features rather than defects.

13.5 The End Grain Wood Floor — Wooden Cobblestones

Think cobblestones. Not stone — wood. The same principle as the ancient stone cobbled
streets of European cities, but made from wooden blocks set with the end grain facing
up. Wood cookies. Wooden cobblestones. Each block is a cross-section of a log or branch
— the rings visible, the cellular structure exposed, the grain perpendicular to the
walking surface. Laid in patterns. Glued. Sanded flat. Oiled. A floor unlike anything
that comes from a showroom.

THE FÖRÄDLINGSVÄRDE — VALUE TRANSFORMATION:

The raw material for this floor is the construction industry's most worthless output:
the short offcut. Too short for structure. Too short for cladding. Too short for
furniture. No resale value. Disposal cost. Negative economic value.

One cross-cut transforms it. The same piece of timber that was heading for the skip
becomes a floor cobble. The piece that costs less than nothing — that you were paying
to remove — becomes a component of a floor that lasts centuries, improves with age,
looks extraordinary, and cannot be bought at any price from a conventional flooring
supplier because it does not exist as a commercial product.

The förädlingsvärde — the Swedish concept of added value through refinement — is
essentially infinite here. The input has negative value. The output has significant
value. The transformation requires one cut and some linseed oil.

Almost nobody in the world finds a use for short timber offcuts. You can use anything
thicker than 30mm — branches pruned from the olive tree in the garden, pallet stringer
offcuts too damaged for structural use, furniture workshop shorts, sawmill end pieces.
All of it becomes floor. From skip to showroom in one pass of a mitre saw.

THE PREPARATION — TIME CONSUMING BUT LOW SKILL:

The end grain floor is not quick. It is time-consuming because of the volume of pieces
required for a floor area. This is something to prepare over weeks in advance — not
on the assembly day. A jig on a workbench, a mitre saw set to the chosen thickness
(30 to 40mm is standard), and an operator feeding offcuts through. One motion, one
cobble. Anyone can do this. The skill level required is essentially zero. Outsource
it cheaply to anyone willing to spend a day cutting, or do it over evenings watching
television.

Pre-assemble in 1m x 1m backing tiles — glue the cobbles onto a backing sheet in
manageable sections. Each tile installs like a large floor tile. The preparation is
the investment. The installation is fast.

Minimum depth: 30 to 40mm. Not less. At 30mm depth with 1mm removed per sanding, the
floor can be renewed thirty times before it is consumed. At one sanding per decade,
that is three hundred years of floor. The thicker the cobble the longer the floor
lasts. The deeper the linseed oil penetrates — soaking into the open end grain cell
structure far more deeply than it would penetrate face grain — the harder and more
water-resistant the surface becomes with each treatment.

This is categorically different from parquet. A parquet floor is face grain timber —
planks laid flat, tongue and groove, essentially compressed sawdust with a thin veneer
layer on top in most commercial versions. It looks like wood. It scratches easily. When
a plank breaks you cannot replace it without disrupting the whole floor. The end grain
floor is solid timber through its full depth. There is no veneer. What you see goes
all the way down. Scratch-proof in the practical sense — scratches compress the cell
tubes rather than abrade across them. The surface self-heals under normal use.

THE WEAR RESISTANCE ARGUMENT:

End grain exposes the cellular tube structure of the timber. Under foot traffic these
tubes compress slightly and spring back — the surface is self-cushioning. The cells
are like drinking straws packed tightly together, end-on. Under load they support each
other laterally just as hexagons in a honeycomb support each other — the force
distributes across the entire cross section through the cell walls. The surface
hardens with use. End grain is three to four times more wear-resistant than face grain
of the same species. Tank factories ran tanks over end grain floors. The more abuse
it takes the more beautiful it becomes.

If one section is damaged beyond what sanding fixes — pry it up. Replace the blocks.
Identical new blocks drop in, glued, sanded flush. The repair is invisible.

INDOOR SPECIFICATION — GLUED AND PLANNED:

For interior living spaces, the blocks are planed to consistent thickness, glued to
a stable substrate — plywood or existing timber floor — and the surface sanded flat
after installation. Finish with multiple coats of boiled linseed oil, each coat
fully absorbed before the next. The oil polymerises inside the cell structure.
The surface becomes hard, warm, and water-resistant.

Square blocks for a geometric grid pattern. Round log cookies — cross-sections of
actual branches with visible rings and natural edge — for a more organic mosaic.
Both can be combined. Mixed species, mixed diameters, mixed ring patterns create a
floor that is genuinely unique — it cannot be replicated because no two trees are
the same.

Walnut end grain paired with the walnut and bronze interior palette described below
produces something extraordinary. The floor, the taps, the lamp housings — all the
same warm dark tones. The limewash walls and linseed-oiled I-joist ceiling provide
contrast. The result looks like it was designed by someone with serious taste and an
unlimited budget. It was designed by someone with a chainsaw and €70 in linseed oil.

OUTDOOR AND GARAGE SPECIFICATION — SAND BED AND OIL:

For garages, workshops, and outdoor terraces: blocks laid on a compacted sand bed
rather than glued. Gaps filled with sand or fine gravel. Finish with boiled linseed
oil or Roslags mahogany treatment — the Scandinavian boat timber treatment of raw
linseed oil, pine tar, and turpentine — which penetrates deeply and provides weather
resistance. On an outdoor surface, oil annually. The floor weathers and darkens
beautifully.

The sand bed method means any block can be individually lifted and replaced without
disturbing adjacent blocks. For a garage where oil spills, heavy tools, and vehicle
weight are the daily reality: end grain on sand is the single best surface available.
Things dropped on it do not crack — the surface absorbs impact. Oil spills soak in
and add to the patina. The floor improves with every working day spent on it.

COST:

Forest branches cut to depth with a chainsaw and depth stop: essentially free.
Timber offcuts from a sawmill: approximately €20 to €40 per m².
Linseed oil for 100m²: approximately €70.
Total for a free-material floor: approximately €70.

13.6 The Interior Design Palette — Walnut and Bronze

The RCP house has a natural material palette that is both ancient and genuinely
sophisticated. The palette emerges from the materials of the system itself — dark
linseed-oiled timber, warm limewash walls, pale whitewashed ceiling boards — and
needs only one or two deliberate additions to become something that looks expensive
and intentional throughout.

Walnut and bronze is the correct addition. Walnut is the darkest, richest common
European timber — deeply grained, warm brown to near-black, extraordinarily beautiful
in end grain. Bronze has a warmth and weight that brass and chrome cannot match — it
is the oldest decorative metal in construction, used continuously for five thousand
years because nothing ages better. Together, against limewash walls and linseed-oiled
pine I-joists, walnut and bronze create an interior that reads as both ancient and
entirely contemporary.

Roslags mahogany exterior treatment — dark, reddish brown, weathered to near-black
over time — provides the exterior version of the same palette. The house reads
consistently from outside to inside.

PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS:

Taps and shower fittings: unlacquered bronze or aged bronze. Available from most
European bathroom suppliers. Cost similar to premium chrome. Develops a patina with
use. Never looks cheap.

Light fittings: bronze pendant lights, bronze wall brackets, bronze floor lamps.
Industrial-style cage fittings in bronze over Edison bulbs. Simple and genuinely
beautiful.

Door handles and hardware: solid bronze or walnut. Rope handles on interior doors
are free and look deliberate.

Shelving and furniture: walnut-veneered boards or solid walnut offcuts for feature
shelving. The end grain floor in walnut blocks if budget allows. Small walnut side
tables, walnut picture frames. Every walnut element reinforces the palette.

The total cost of the walnut and bronze additions — taps, light fittings, a few
pieces of hardware — is €500 to €1,500 depending on choices. The visual impact
relative to cost is disproportionate. Bronze hardware in a limewash room with a
linseed-oiled timber ceiling looks like it belongs in an architectural magazine.
Because it does.

13.6 Outdoor Surfaces

The outdoor surface philosophy follows the same principle as the house: natural,
low-maintenance, improving with age, zero or near-zero cost where possible.

RESIN BOUND GRAVEL — DEFAULT: Aggregate mixed with two-part polyurethane resin,
raked flat, left to cure. Completely DIY. No specialist tools beyond a mixing paddle
and a flat rake. Any aggregate works — local gravel, crushed stone, sea pebbles,
coloured glass. Permeable to rainwater. No puddles. No mud. Bonds to any stable base.
Cost approximately €15 to €25 per square metre in materials. Looks expensive. Lasts
decades. Zero maintenance.

CREEPING THYME AS LIVING SURFACE: Between the gravel paths and around the terrace,
creeping thyme as ground cover. Drought-tolerant after establishment in the Algarve.
Self-seeding. Fragrant when walked on. Soft underfoot. Flowers attract pollinators.
Zero water after year one.

PALLET DECK BOARDS AS DECKING: Disassemble surplus pallets, treat boards with pine
tar, screw to a simple timber frame on adjustable feet. A fully demountable terrace.
Zero material cost if pallets are available. Warm timber surface.

DRY LAID STONE: Flat stones set directly on compacted sand. No mortar. Completely
reversible. Permeable. Free if stone is on site. Typical Algarve rural aesthetic.

PEBBLE MOSAIC: River or beach pebbles set in lime mortar. Portuguese traditional
technique. Free material. Labour intensive. Lasts indefinitely.

END GRAIN ROUNDS OUTDOORS: Larger diameter trunk sections set vertically as stepping
stones. Forest material. Free. The same end grain principle as the interior floor —
weathers beautifully outdoors.

WHAT DOES NOT BELONG: Concrete paving. Tarmac. Ceramic tile on adhesive. These are
permanent, impermeable, hot in summer, and against the entire philosophy of this system.

14.4 Storage as Architecture

The enemy of a beautiful home is clutter. Clutter is not a lifestyle problem. It is
a design problem. There is never enough storage in a conventionally designed home
because storage space does not appear in the listing as a selling feature. Bedrooms
do. So storage is squeezed out in favour of rooms that photograph well.

The Hestia Home inverts this. Storage is designed into the architecture, not added
as furniture after the fact.

THE WALL AS CUPBOARD:

Two RCP panels back to back — two rooms sharing a wall — creates a wall assembly
approximately 540mm deep when storage is built into both faces. Open it from one
room to access a wardrobe. Open it from the other room to access shelving. The
storage is inside the structure. It takes no floor space from either room.

This wall is also an exceptional acoustic barrier. A 540mm assembly filled with
clothing, linen, and soft goods on both faces absorbs sound at every frequency.
The same wall that stores your clothing simultaneously provides acoustic separation
between bedroom and living room that no standard partition wall can match. One
element. Multiple functions. The permaculture principle applied to architecture.

THE WALK-IN WARDROBE: Full height. Proper hanging rail. Shelving with space around

THE WALK-IN PANTRY: Everything visible at once. Nothing crammed behind something
else. Adjustable shelves. You never run out of something you did not know you had.

THE VESTIBULE (tambur): A transition space between the front door and the main
living area. Outdoor clothes come off here. Shoes are stored here. The dirty interface
is contained. The rest of the house remains clean. This feature disappeared from
modern design because it reduced the headline square footage number on the listing.
It is being brought back because it makes daily life substantially more pleasant.

THE UTILITY ROOM: Cleaning equipment, vacuum, mop, iron. A proper room with a door
where these things live out of sight.

14.5 The Pocket Door in the Pallet Wall

A standard EPAL pallet has three compartments between the stringers, each approximately
200mm wide running the full 1200mm depth of the pallet. By removing the spacer blocks
from one compartment before panel assembly, you create a cavity exactly suited to
receive a sliding pocket door. The door slides horizontally into the pallet void and
disappears into the wall when open.

The pallet structure on either side of the empty compartment provides the structural
framing for the pocket. The OSB skins provide the surfaces for the door track. Zero
additional materials beyond the door hardware. Plan it at fabrication — identify which
panels receive pocket doors, remove the relevant spacer blocks, reinforce adjacent
stringers if needed.

Pocket doors eliminate the floor space consumed by a swinging door arc. In small rooms
and bathrooms this is significant. They also feel deliberate and refined — the door
disappears rather than interrupting the room.

Solid heavy doors throughout. No hollow-core doors anywhere in the house. A hollow
door sounds like one and communicates everything about the quality of a space in the
moment it is touched.

14.6 Layered Lighting and the Smart Default

No central overhead fixtures. A single overhead source flattens a room and produces
the institutional ambience of a corridor or an office.

Layered lighting means: ambient light from wall-mounted or indirect sources. Task
light at specific work positions. Accent light that highlights texture, a shelf, a
plant. The layers combine to create depth and atmosphere.

All lights on Shelly dimmers. Colour temperature controllable — warm in the morning,
neutral at midday, warm again at night. The circadian rhythm follows the light without
the occupant thinking about it. The smart home schedules transitions automatically.

The floating bed with under-lighting. LED strip facing the floor from beneath the bed
frame. The bed appears to float. The light is warm and indirect. At night it provides

USBC OUTLETS EVERYWHERE: Every socket position has a USB-C port alongside the

Phone charging at the bedside — no cable clutter. The USB-C standard is now universal.
Planning for it at build stage costs approximately €8 to €15 extra per position.

RECESSED SOCKETS: Socket boxes set 40mm back from the wall face so the socket sits
flush or slightly behind the surface. Furniture pushes completely flush against the

THE SMART DEFAULT: When you move into the Hestia Home it is already configured.

14.12 The Land — The Permaculture Principle

The house is designed to require minimum maintenance and improve with age. The land
around it should be designed the same way.

The standard: go away for two years and come back to find it better than it was before.

15.6 Complexity Is Poor Design

The best part is no part. Every component you add brings with it maintenance requirements,
failure modes, cost, and interactions with other components that create problems you did
not anticipate. Complexity is not sophistication. It is poor system design.

A conventional house build is the perfect illustration of this principle in reverse. Pipes
running everywhere. Trades that depend on other trades. Systems that interact in ways
nobody fully understands. When something goes wrong — and something always goes wrong —
it is impossible to isolate the cause. The contractor charges to fix the problem they
created. You have no recourse.

Every decision in this system that reduces component count reduces risk, cost, maintenance,
and potential failure simultaneously. The composting toilet has no soil pipe, no tank, no
pump, no connection fee, and no monthly bill. Four separate problems eliminated by the
absence of one system. The one wet wall concentrates all wet services in one location —
if a pipe needs attention, you know exactly where it is.

This principle applies across the entire build. Fewer connections. Fewer interactions.
Fewer things that can go wrong. The system is not simple because it is cheap. It is cheap
because it is simple. Simplicity is the design goal.

PLUMBING — THE PRE-ASSEMBLED WET WALL

The wet wall is the most consequential plumbing decision in the system. A water leak inside
a timber structure is a serious problem. This is where professional involvement is strongly
recommended regardless of build pathway.

The wet wall concept: kitchen and bathroom placed back to back on one internal panel. All
water supply lines, waste pipes, and connections centralised in one location. One water
inlet entering the building. One greywater waste outlet leaving. One hot water supply from
the solar thermal tank.

NO INSULATION IN THE WET WALL CAVITY: The wet wall is left open — uninsulated,
unobstructed, fully accessible. Insulation in the wet wall would trap moisture, obstruct
repairs, and make pipe access needlessly difficult. The thermal penalty of one uninsulated
panel in a fully insulated house is negligible.

PIPES IN PIPES — STANDARD SPECIFICATION: Every water supply pipe runs inside a
protective outer sleeve — a flexible inner pipe inside a larger diameter outer pipe.
Standard practice across most of Western Europe, not yet universal in Portugal. If the
inner pipe fails, the water is contained within the outer sleeve and directed rather than
dispersed into the structure. Specify explicitly in the contractor brief.

THE DRAIN HOLE: A 50mm hole through the base of the wet wall sill — sealed around with
flexible sealant to prevent moisture wicking into the timber — connects to a short stub
pipe leading to the exterior drainage channel that carries roof runoff away from the
house. Any water that enters the wet wall cavity from any source runs by gravity to the
base and exits immediately to the outside. No accumulation. No structural contact. Passive
and permanent.

THE LARGE ACCESS PANEL: The entire inner face of the wet wall — or a substantial section
of it — is a large removable panel fixed with visible screws at regular intervals. Remove
the screws, slide the panel aside, and the entire wet wall interior is open. Every pipe,
every joint, every connection, every fitting visible and reachable by hand. Tap washer
failed, joint weeping, valve seized — open the panel, fix it, replace the panel. Thirty
minutes. No damage to any wall finish. No trace of the repair.

THE WATER SENSOR: A Shelly water leak sensor or Zigbee-compatible equivalent sits at
the base of the wet wall cavity just above the drain hole. The moment any water reaches
the base a notification arrives on the homeowner's phone via the Home Assistant or
Shelly app. The complete monitoring sequence for any leak: water drips from the failed
joint, runs down the cavity, reaches the sensor, notification sent, water exits through
the drain hole. The homeowner is alerted before any accumulation, before any structural
contact, before any damage.

RECOMMENDED APPROACH — PLUMBER PRE-ASSEMBLES THE WET WALL: The wet wall is fully
assembled by a licensed plumber before house assembly begins — either off-site in a
workshop or on-site before the wall panels go up. All supply lines, waste runs, isolation
valves, and connection points are installed and pressure tested as a complete unit. On
assembly day: place the wet wall in position, connect the main water supply, connect
drainage. Plumbing done. A skilled plumber can complete one wet wall in half a day. If
building multiple units, several wet walls can be assembled in one plumber day.

Modern push-fit or press-fit connection systems — Speedfit or equivalent — eliminate
soldering. Click connections, pressure tested, fast to assemble.

LICENSED PLUMBER SCOPE regardless of approach: mains water connection and stopcock,
pressure testing of complete system, solar thermal connections and pressure relief valve,
ANQIP certification. Time: approximately 3 to 4 hours for sign-off.

THERMAL SYSTEM — THREE TIERS:

TIER ONE — DEFAULT MVP:
Ariston Kairos 200L thermosyphon solar hot water system. Approximately €1,500. No pump.
No electricity. No moving parts. Natural circulation driven by temperature differential
between the roof collector and the tank. Provides hot water year-round in southern Europe.
Simple, proven, off the shelf. Install and forget.

TIER TWO — PARABOLIC SOLAR ARRAY WITH SAND BATTERY:
A motorised solar parabolic trough array — available as a complete buyable unit at
approximately €1,500 to €3,000 — tracks the sun and superheats a pressurised glycol
loop to 80 to 150°C. This is dramatically hotter than a flat plate collector can achieve.
The difference between lukewarm water and high-grade thermal energy capable of running
a home.

That superheated loop feeds pipes embedded in a 300mm sand layer — either as an
insulated storage vessel inside the house or as a sand foundation layer with EPS
insulation below it. The sand stores the heat. Research from the University of Kassel
and the Solar Fire initiative confirms that a well-insulated thermal store retains over
90 percent of its energy for more than 24 hours. In the Algarve with 300 days of direct
sun, the sand charges continuously. The result: passive underfloor heating that runs
for weeks without direct sun. In southern Europe this means warm floors throughout the
year with zero electricity consumption for heating.

Eliminating heating from the electrical load means the battery bank can be dramatically
smaller and the PV array can be smaller. The system pays for itself through the
reduction in electrical infrastructure.

George Plhak's open source work at georgesworkshop.blogspot.com provides practical
build and installation documentation for parabolic troughs validated over years of
real-world testing.

TIER THREE — PASSIVE COOLING: TWO ANCIENT TECHNIQUES:

THE CANADIAN WELL:
Pipes buried 1.5 to 2 metres underground, running from outside the house footprint
to an air intake inside. Ground temperature in the Algarve at that depth: approximately
17 to 18°C year-round. In peak summer when air temperature reaches 38 to 42°C, air
drawn through the buried pipes enters the house at 17 to 18°C — a passive cooling
effect of 20°C with zero electricity beyond a small circulation fan. No compressor.
No refrigerant. No running cost. The Romans and Greeks used the same principle with
underground water channels cooling incoming air — the same physics, earlier
implementation. The Canadian well is the modern name for an ancient technique.

The pipes are buried during the foundation phase — essentially free to add at that
point, very expensive to retrofit later. Plan it in from the start.

THE SOLAR CHIMNEY:
A vertical channel on the south face of the house, clad in black-painted material and
covered with glass. Solar radiation heats the black surface to extreme temperatures.
The air inside the channel heats, rises, and exits through a vent at the top. The
rising hot air creates a strong upward draft — a suction that draws air from inside
the house upward and out.

The two systems work together as a complete passive cooling circuit. The solar chimney
creates suction at ceiling level — pulling the hottest air in the house upward and out.
The Canadian well supplies cool 17 to 18°C air at floor level — drawn in passively by
the suction the chimney creates. Cool dense air enters at floor level. Hot air exits
at ceiling level. The occupant lives in the cool lower layer. The ceiling layer is
continuously flushed without any mechanical system running.

Combined with the rockwool roof insulation blocking solar heat gain through the roof
— the dominant summer heat source in the Algarve — the complete passive cooling system:

One — insulated roof blocks incoming solar heat.
Two — solar chimney exhausts accumulated ceiling heat.
Three — Canadian well supplies cool floor-level replacement air.
Four — ceiling fan if needed for air distribution — 15 to 30 watts.

No compressor. No refrigerant. No running cost beyond a small fan. Zero electricity
for cooling in a climate that reaches 40°C in summer.

Combined result of Tier Two and Three: the house never needs active heating or cooling.
Hot underfloor from solar thermal storage. Cool fresh air from passive systems. The
only electrical loads are lighting, devices, fridge, and small circulation pumps. The
PV system and battery bank required for this is minimal — perhaps 3 to 4 panels and a
modest battery rather than a full off-grid system.

The complete abundant off-grid house — parabolic array, sand storage, Canadian well,
solar chimney, small PV array — runs indefinitely with no grid connection and no
monthly energy bill. The investment is front-loaded. The running cost is zero.

17.2 The Workshop as Quality Assurance

The most common criticism of this system is that a DIY builder without construction experience

The workshop is the answer to that criticism.

The workshop exists primarily so that the DIY builder has fabricated panels correctly under

The sequence is: attend a workshop first, build your own house second. In the workshop you
learn the moisture protocol by doing it. You see what correct adhesive coverage looks like when
spread. You perform the peel test and see OSB fibres tear rather than a clean bond line peel.
You understand why the stringer positions are marked before the OSB is lowered. None of this
requires prior construction experience. It requires having done it once under supervision.

This is not a new concept. Tradespeople serve apprenticeships for the same reason. The workshop

quality of the finished house. Everything else in the system is self-evident assembly. The SIP

18.3 The Grassroots Movement

THE INCENTIVE PROBLEM WITH HIRED LABOUR:

There are two ways to hire a contractor and both produce misaligned incentives.

Fixed price: the contractor agrees a total sum before work begins. Their incentive
from that moment is to finish as fast as possible using the cheapest methods that
still pass inspection. Every shortcut that goes undetected increases their margin.
The client's interest and the contractor's interest are directly opposed from the
moment the contract is signed.

Hourly rate: the contractor is paid for time. Their incentive is to extend the time.
Complexity is profitable. Problems that require investigation are profitable. The
slower the work proceeds the more money they make. Again, directly opposed interests.

There is no hiring arrangement where the contractor's incentive aligns with the
client's interest. The misalignment is structural, not personal.

The barn-raising community build changes the incentive structure completely. Nobody
is being paid by the hour or by the project. Everyone present wants the house to be
good because they know the person who will live in it. Social capital — reputation,
relationship, reciprocity — replaces financial incentive. The person who does a bad
job is the person who let their friend down. That is a stronger motivator than any
contract clause.

The person who builds your house with you today is the person whose house you will
build next year. The quality of their work is the standard they set for what you will
receive in return.

Once you have built one house with this system you will not stop. Your friends will ask

18.4 The Foundation Spectrum

REMOVABLE STONE OR CONCRETE PADS: Large flat stones or precast concrete blocks placed at
load points. Zero cost if stones are available on site. No excavation. No specialist
equipment. Ancient method — houses have been built this way for centuries. Fully demountable.
No trace on removal. This is how timber frame buildings were founded before concrete existed.
Appropriate for Positions 1 and 2.

EARTH SCREWS — DEFAULT FOR ALL BUILDS: Helical ground anchors installed in one day by one
operator with a compact hydraulic machine. Drill a pilot hole first, then drive the screw
to depth. No soil preparation required beyond the pilot hole. Rocky ground adds some
difficulty — large rocks may require repositioning the screw slightly — but this is
manageable in almost all rural terrain. The installer knows what to do. Foundation sizing —
number of screws, spacing, depth — is the installer's professional expertise. The load is
light and the screws are massively over-specified for a single storey timber frame. Cost
approximately €2,000 to €3,000 for a family home. Fully removable and reusable. Engineer-
certifiable from manufacturer published load data. Default for all five legal positions.

EXISTING CONCRETE SLAB: Many rural plots in Portugal have an existing foundation — a ruin,
a demolished outbuilding, an old slab — with nothing on top. This is an ideal situation.
Clean the surface, lay the sill plate, build the house. No new foundation cost. The existing
slab is already registered. The house that sits on it inherits that status.

PAD FOOTINGS: Small concrete pads at load points only where bedrock prevents earth screw
installation. No continuous slab. Approximately €1,500 to €2,500.

THE SILL BEAM: Three 75x225mm treated structural timber sill beams — a 3x9 inch sill —
placed across the foundation points bridge any surface irregularity. A beam of this section
does not deflect under the loads of a single storey timber frame. A few foundation points
approximately level is sufficient — the beam does the precision work. Panels erected on top
of this beam are level by definition.

THE CAR JACK ARGUMENT: The complete structure is so lightweight that a standard 5-tonne
car jack can lift an entire corner of the house. If a foundation point settles, shifts, or
needs replacement after years of use — jack the corner, reposition or replace the support,
lower the house back down. No heavy machinery. No structural engineer required for this
operation. The kind of maintenance that in a conventional masonry house requires expensive
underpinning is here a two-hour job with a tool available at any hardware shop.

19. CLIMATE ADAPTATIONS — THE SAME SYSTEM, ANYWHERE IN THE WORLD
================================================================================

19.1 The Upgrade Is Additive

The system described in this thesis is optimised for the Algarve — zero snow load, mild
winters, 3,000 hours of sunshine per year. The same system adapts to any climate in the
world through two changes only: wall depth and I-joist depth. Everything else — the
fabrication protocol, the jig, the nail types, the adhesive, the assembly sequence — is
identical. The upgrade is additive, not structural.

19.2 Single Pallet Wall — More Than Sufficient for Southern Europe

The single 145mm pallet wall with blown rockwool achieves approximately 0.39 to 0.42
W/m²K (see Appendix L for full ISO 6946 calculation). Standard Portuguese brick and
mortar wall: approximately 1.8 W/m²K. The single pallet wall is 4 to 5 times better
insulated than the conventional Portuguese standard.

The 0.25 W/m²K regulatory target is a northern European cold climate standard — Sweden,
Norway, Germany. It is not a Portuguese requirement and not the right benchmark for the
Algarve. In Portugal, the difference between 0.40 W/m²K and 1.8 W/m²K is the difference
between needing air conditioning most of the summer and not needing it. Just the fact
that the wall is insulated at all makes a massive difference to energy bills and comfort
in this climate.

A note on thermal bridging: the parallel-path calculation treats the wall as a uniform
mixture of timber and insulation. In reality the timber stringers conduct heat faster
than the rockwool between them — a minor thermal bridge at each stringer position. The
true U-value is marginally worse than calculated. In the Portuguese climate this is not
a meaningful concern. In cold climates the double pallet wall with offset stringers
addresses this directly — no continuous timber path runs through the full wall depth.

19.3 Double Pallet Walls — Passive House Territory

Two EPAL pallets screwed face to face before panel fabrication doubles the wall core
depth from 145mm to 290mm. The OSB skins are the same. The adhesive and nailing
protocol is the same. The jig widens to accommodate the deeper core. Everything else
is identical.

The verified U-value of the double pallet wall: approximately 0.21 to 0.23 W/m²K —
which meets the Swedish Passive House target of 0.25 W/m²K and exceeds it.

Panel bending stiffness increases with the cube of the distance between skin centroids.
Going from 145mm to 290mm core increases the centroid distance from approximately
160mm to 305mm. Stiffness ratio: (305/160)³ = approximately 7x stiffer. The double
pallet wall is not twice as stiff. It is seven times as stiff in bending — dramatically
better wind resistance for exposed sites or taller buildings.

The cost of upgrading to double pallet walls per panel:
Three extra pallets at €17 each: €51
Proportional additional rockwool: approximately €18
Total additional cost per panel: approximately €65 to €70

For a full 26-panel house: approximately €1,700 to €1,800 total upgrade cost.
Against a total build cost of €35,000 that is a five percent premium for a wall that
goes from 0.40 to 0.22 W/m²K — meeting Passive House standard for any climate.

Secondary benefits of the double pallet wall:
— Any screw driven anywhere into the wall face hits solid timber. No stud finding.
  No toggle bolts. Heavy kitchen units and shelving fix directly at any position.
— Mass and depth of 290mm wall provides exceptional acoustic performance —
  a genuine benefit for anyone building near roads, neighbours, or noise sources.
— Offset stringers in the two pallets eliminate any continuous thermal bridge
  through the full wall depth — superior to single pallet in cold climates.

19.4 Deeper I-Joists for Cold Climate Roofs

The roof I-joist depth determines insulation cavity depth. STEICO manufactures I-joists
up to 400mm depth. 220mm gives 220mm of rockwool. 240mm gives 240mm. The assembly
sequence is identical regardless of depth.

For cold climates: 220mm or 240mm I-joists filled with blown rockwool, combined with
double pallet walls, produces a complete building envelope at Passive House standard
without additional systems or complexity.

20.4 Extending the House — Drag and Drop

A family without children builds a 7m x 8m house. Three years later there are two

THE DRAG AND DROP EXTENSION:

The end wall of the house is not load-bearing. It is a closure panel. Every connection
is a screw. To add one room:

Unscrew one section of steel roof sheet at the end — two minutes, a cordless drill.
Unscrew the corner connections. Unscrew the sill beam connection. The entire end wall
lifts away in one piece — drag it ten metres clear. The house is temporarily open at
one end, sealed overnight with a tarpaulin if needed.

Drive new ground screws at the extension footprint. Lay new sill beams. Install new
floor joists and platform. Erect new RCP panels fabricated in advance on the same jig.
Connect the new roof section. Replace the end wall at the new end position. One to two
days for a team.

No breaking. No rubble. No structural temporary works. No disruption to occupied rooms.
The original end wall is reused exactly — unscrewed, moved, rescrewed.

PLANNING AHEAD FOR EXTENSION:

Buy roof steel sheet to the eventual extended length at the initial build. The marginal
cost is small. The time saved at extension is substantial — no new sheet, no join detail.

Drive earth screws at the planned extension footprint during the initial build.

Leave end wall connections accessible — no permanent sealant that makes removal
difficult.

A conventional house extension costs €50,000 to €150,000 and takes six months.
This extension costs the price of the new panels, floor structure, and ground screws —
approximately €3,000 to €5,000 in materials — and takes one to two days.

THE THREE DELIVERY MODES:

ONE — BUILD ENTIRELY ON SITE: Standard method. Foundation first, panels fabricated
on site over preceding weeks, erected on assembly day.

TWO — PREFABRICATE PANELS OFF SITE: Panels made in a workshop or by a licensed
builder, delivered flat, erected on site. Professional certification possible. Same
assembly sequence, different fabrication location.

THREE — COMPLETE HOUSE DELIVERED BY CRANE: Build the complete house off site.
Deliver and place by crane. Either as climate shell only or fully finished house.
Standard practice across Europe for mobile and modular construction.

For houses above 5 tonnes finished weight: build in two halves, crane each half into
position, join on site using standard panel-to-panel connection detail.

THE TWO ROOF TYPES:

MONO-PITCH — SHED ROOF (pulpettak in Swedish): One direction of slope. Standard
specification for this system. Simple, one plane, no ridge, no valley. Front wall
earth screws set 300mm higher than back wall earth screws — slope built into the
foundation setting. Interior ceiling flat. Already described throughout this thesis.

DOUBLE PITCH — SADDLE ROOF (sadeltak in Swedish): Two shed roofs placed face to
face — two standard shoebox modules with their front walls touching, forming a
central load-bearing wall at the ridge. Each half is a standard 7m x 6m mono-pitch
module. Together they form a 7m x 12m house with a conventional pitched roof
appearance and dramatic interior volume at the ridge. The central wall carries the
I-joist roof load from both sides. No ridge beam required. The I-joists from each
half rest on the central wall double top plate. Same panel specification throughout.

Standard modules:
Half module — 7m x 6m = 42m². One shoebox. Mono-pitch roof.
Full module — 7m x 12m = 84m². Two shoeboxes face to face. Saddle roof with central
load-bearing wall.
Extended module — add 1.25m increments to either end for additional rooms.

THE RELOCATABLE COMPLETE HOUSE:

A 30m² ultra-lightweight RCP house — designed for maximum strength-to-weight ratio —
weighs approximately 2,500 to 3,000kg. A standard 5-tonne hired mobile crane lifts it
comfortably with margin. This enables three-position demountability:

ONE — Disassemble into flat panels and transport on a trailer.
TWO — Lift the climate shell as one unit by crane and place on new ground screws.
THREE — Lift the fully finished house as one unit if weight permits.

THE STRUCTURAL REQUIREMENTS FOR CRANE LIFTING:

The sill beams form a continuous rigid perimeter frame — bolted at all corners with
steel brackets, not just toe-screwed. This prevents the base from racking during lift.

Floor beams — 2x6 or 2x8 solid timber — span across the sill frame with short
blocking between them at mid-span. The floor becomes a rigid stressed-skin platform.
This is the enabling component — without it the house would sag and distort under
asymmetric crane load.

The double top plate running continuously across all wall panels ties the top of the
structure and distributes the crane load across all four corners simultaneously.

Four corner posts — heavy section solid timber or LVL — run from sill beam to roof
level at each corner. Each has a certified lifting hook at the top. Four hooks total.
The crane attaches to all four. The house lifts as one solid unit.

Ground screws are pre-installed on the destination site before the house arrives.
The house is lowered onto the screw head plates and bolted. Connect utilities. Done.

EXTENDING UPWARD — ADDING A SECOND STOREY:

The wall panels carry well under 10 percent of their compression capacity at single
storey. Adding a full second storey increases the compression demand but remains within
comfortable limits. The roof comes off first — steel sheet and I-joists removed,
all reusable. A second floor platform is built on the existing wall top plates. Second
storey RCP panels are erected on the platform using the same jig and protocol. The
roof goes back on at the new height. One to two days of weather exposure, manageable
with tarpaulins in the Algarve summer.

20.5 Multi-Storey Construction — Open Research Question

The structural analysis in Chapter 6 addresses single storey construction only. The
wall compression utilisation at single storey is well under 10 percent — the panels could carry
a three storey building before other engineering considerations govern. For two
storeys the utilisation rises to approximately 5.7 percent. For three storeys
approximately 11.4 percent. Compression capacity is not the limiting factor for multi-
storey construction.

The governing engineering questions for two and three storey RCP construction are:
— Wind racking resistance of the complete structure under increased wind moment
— Floor-to-wall connection detail at each level
— Lateral stability system — stairwell core, cross walls, diaphragm action
— Foundation upgrade — earth screws may require closer spacing or larger diameter
   for multi-storey loads

These are solvable engineering problems. They require a proper structural analysis that
does not currently exist. Multi-storey RCP construction is identified as a priority
research direction for the prototype programme. The tall orientation 2500mm panel with
deck boards acting as vertical compression columns is the natural candidate for ground
floor walls in a two storey build — maximum compression stiffness where loads are
highest.

20.5 Cold Climate Adaptation — Open Research Question

The double pallet wall with blown rockwool achieves approximately 0.21 to 0.23 W/m²K —
within Passive House envelope territory. Combined with 240mm or 300mm I-joist roof
cavity filled with blown rockwool, the complete building envelope approaches Passive
House certification without any additional system complexity.

Open questions for cold climate deployment:
— What additional detailing is required at junctions for airtightness in cold climates?
— What is the practical northern limit for this system without mechanical ventilation?
— How does the thermal bridging at stringer positions affect real-world performance
   versus the calculated parallel-path U-value in cold climates?
— What modification to the earth screw specification is needed for frost heave zones?

These questions are best answered through prototype builds in northern European climates.
The system logic is identical. The detailing at junctions requires climate-specific
development.

20.6 The Aircrete RCP Variant — Further Application Research

The Concrete Oreo basement panel described in Chapter 7.3 opens a separate research
direction for surface applications beyond basements. The aircrete-filled RCP is a
different product from the standard rockwool RCP — it trades lightweight handleability
for mass, fire resistance, thermal storage, and indefinite rot resistance.

Specific applications worth investigating through prototype:

FIRE-RATED PARTY WALLS: An aircrete-filled RCP between two apartments or units provides
fire separation and acoustic isolation in a single assembly without a separate fire wall.

SOUND ISOLATION: The combination of mass and rigidity in the aircrete-filled RCP would
produce exceptional acoustic performance — relevant for music rooms, studios, or any
application where sound transmission between spaces is critical.

HOT CLIMATE THERMAL MASS: In climates with large day-night temperature swings the
thermal mass of the aircrete core dampens interior temperature variation. The wall
absorbs heat during the day and releases it at night — reducing or eliminating the
need for active cooling.

SECURE STORAGE: The combination of structural rigidity, fire resistance, and mass makes
the aircrete-filled RCP a candidate for secure rooms or vaults within a larger structure.

FULL DECK BOARD PALLET UPGRADE: A construction-specific pallet manufactured with full
continuous deck boards — no logistics gaps — would increase the vertical timber cross
section by approximately 26 percent at minimal additional cost per pallet. Combined with
18mm OSB skins, closer nail spacing, and structural screws, this represents the natural
component upgrade path for multi-storey applications. The fabrication protocol and
assembly sequence are identical. This is the same system with denser components.

The direct market competitors for aircrete-filled RCP panels are autoclaved aerated
concrete block — AAC, brands such as Ytong — at approximately €100 to €150 per cubic
metre, and hempcrete at approximately €80 to €150 per cubic metre. The aircrete RCP
is distinguishable from both by its timber skeleton, reversibility, and site-fabrication
from widely available materials.

20.7 Further RCP Applications

The standard RCP and its variants — standard rockwool fill, aircrete fill, marine plywood
skin — open a range of applications beyond the primary family home use case.

THE RCP POOL:

A swimming pool or plunge pool built from marine plywood RCP panels is a genuinely
practical application. At 1250mm internal depth the pool is ideal for children and adults
— deep enough to swim in, shallow enough for standing. Panels are fabricated as standard
but with marine plywood skins replacing OSB on both faces. The interior face is sealed
with epoxy resin — the same marine epoxy used on boat hulls. The result is a watertight,
rot-proof, structurally rigid pool shell.

Finish options for the interior pool surface:
— Epoxy alone: smooth, clear or tinted, easy to apply
— Pebbles or river stones set into final epoxy coat: natural texture, traditional look
— Fibreglass overlay on epoxy: additional watertight layer, standard pool surface
— Concrete render over epoxy primer: traditional pool finish, any colour

The pool can be above ground, semi-buried, or fully buried. For a buried pool the marine
plywood outer face with bituminous membrane handles groundwater pressure. Standard RCP
panel lifting rings allow the pool to be disassembled and relocated if needed.

Insulation: the standard pallet core with blown rockwool maintains water temperature
significantly better than conventional uninsulated concrete pools. Connect the solar
thermal collector already installed for domestic hot water to a pool heat exchanger and
the pool is passively heated through the summer with zero running cost. An insulated
pool with solar thermal input stays swimmable in the Algarve for eight to ten months of
the year without any active heating system.

THE GARDEN STUDIO, SAUNA, OR STORAGE CUBE:

A single RCP panel is 2500mm x 1250mm. A cube built from six panels — four walls, one
floor panel, one roof panel — produces a 1250mm x 1250mm x 2500mm structure. This is a
useful storage unit, a tool shed, or a cold-weather sauna at near-zero material cost
using reclaimed pallets.

A slightly larger structure — four panels wide, four panels long, three panels tall —
produces a 5m x 5m garden studio, garage, or workshop at very low cost. Marine plywood
skins and epoxy for a sauna. Standard OSB and rockwool for a workshop or garage.

THE CONFIDENCE BUILDER — START WITH A SHED:

The single most useful preparation for building a family home with this system is to
first build something small. A garden shed. A sauna. A single-car garage. A workshop.
The skills, the jig geometry, the fabrication sequence, the connection details — all are
identical to the full house. The only difference is scale.

Build the shed alone first. Learn the jig. Learn the adhesive timing. Learn the ratchet
strap clamping. Make the mistakes on a structure where the stakes are low. Then when the
house build begins, every step is familiar.

Build the sauna with a neighbour. They contribute their time, you contribute yours. You
learn together. The neighbour's mistakes are different from your mistakes — between two
people the combined knowledge compounds faster than either learns alone.

If a carpenter is available for even one day, invite them. Not to do the work — to watch
and comment. A carpenter who has spent years working with timber will notice details that
a first-time builder cannot see. Which grain direction to orient the boards. Where to put
the first nail. Why the jig needs a small taper. Practical knowledge accumulated over a
career that cannot be written down — but can be transmitted in a morning of working
side by side.

The barn-raising tradition is not nostalgia. It is an efficient knowledge transfer
protocol. Whoever builds this first in a community cannot stop talking about it. The
second build in that community will be faster and better because of what the first
builder learned and shared. That is how skills spread.

21.1 Absence of Physical Prototype Data

A master's thesis does not require a built prototype. That is precisely what a thesis is for —
it establishes the analytical and specification foundation that makes a prototype worth building
and its results interpretable. Every engineering innovation follows this sequence: analytical
proof first, physical validation second. This thesis is the first step. The prototype is the
second.

The prototype is the obvious and intended next step. This thesis exists to make it meaningful.
A prototype built before the system is fully specified produces ambiguous results — if something
fails, it is impossible to know whether the fault lies in the design, the specification, or the
execution. This thesis defines all three precisely. Every material, every connection, every
fabrication step, every acceptance criterion is specified in sufficient detail that when the
prototype is built, every result is interpretable against a defined baseline.

The response to "there is no prototype" is: correct. This thesis is why the prototype will be
worth building. Without a defined specification, a prototype is just an experiment. With this
specification, it is a validation.

Moreover, every component in this system is already independently validated. The EPAL pallet
carries a certified dynamic load of 1,500 kg — validated through industrial logistics
applications across Europe for decades. The OSB3 is CE marked to EN 300, with published
structural properties under Eurocode 5. The CE-marked I-joist comes with manufacturer span
tables derived from extensive laboratory testing. The earth screws have engineer-certifiable
load data from manufacturer testing. The Sikaflex 512 has published bond strength data from
structural glazing and marine applications where failure is catastrophic. Every component has
been validated independently for loads that far exceed what this system demands. The prototype
will confirm what overengineered components already predict. It will not produce surprises.

The structural analysis in Chapter 6 provides strong analytical grounds for confidence. The
utilisation ratios are comfortable. The mutual restraint between OSB skins and pallet core
means the system has inherent redundancy — the bond does not need to be perfect because the
mechanical fasteners provide independent structural connection, and the stack weight and ratchet
straps during cure provide supplementary clamping. Three independent systems where any one
alone would be sufficient. A structure this redundant does not fail quietly.

21.2 Thermal Performance Estimates

The thermal performance of the pallet-core panel and the I-joist roof assembly are estimated
from first principles using published thermal conductivity values. Actual performance should be
measured in physical specimens using the hot box method per EN ISO 8990.

21.3 Pallet Quality Variability

The grading protocol defined in Chapter 5 addresses the most significant sources of variability,
but verification through systematic testing of graded pallets under representative load
conditions is required before the protocol can be considered fully validated.

21.4 Connection Detail Specification

The connection details require formal connection schedule development for the engineer submission
package. In particular, the surface preparation requirement for the Sikaflex bond — clean, dry,
free from contamination — needs to be explicitly included as a step in the construction protocol.

21.5 Water Extraction Licensing

Stream pump water extraction requires a licence from the Agencia Portuguesa do Ambiente. This
requirement adds complexity that varies by location and watercourse classification.

21.6 I-Joist Availability and Pricing in Portugal

While I-joists are widely available across Northern Europe, their availability and current
pricing at Leroy Merlin Portimao or equivalent Algarve suppliers requires direct verification
before finalising the cost analysis for a specific project.

21.7 Regulatory Analysis

The legal framework analysis in Chapter 18 is indicative rather than definitive. A thorough
legal analysis by a Portuguese planning law specialist is required before the system can be
confidently recommended for specific deployment contexts.

21.8 THE INSPECTION SYSTEM AND TWO-STAGE VIEWING
================================================================================

The RCP system's demountable nature creates an inspection capability that no conventional
house can offer. This deserves formal description as both a quality assurance tool and
a sales asset.

THE ACCESS HATCH SYSTEM:

Every RCP panel has one standardised circular inspection hatch — a 100mm diameter hole
drilled through the gypsum and inner OSB, fitted with a screw-on plastic cap. Position:
centre of each panel at 300mm from the bottom — above the salt layer, below the
electrical conduit zone. The cap unscrews in seconds. A moisture meter probe goes in.
A phone camera goes in. A nose goes near it — you can smell rot, mould, or moisture
before any instrument confirms it.

THE SENSOR NETWORK:

A cheap microcontroller with a temperature and humidity sensor — approximately €3 to
€5 per unit — installed inside each wall cavity before the inner OSB closes. Connected
to the Home Assistant smart home system via the house WiFi. The sensor reports wall
cavity conditions continuously from the day the house is built. If humidity rises above
a threshold, the smart home alerts the owner. The sensor log provides a complete
history of every wall cavity from installation.

A small camera in the roof void provides visual access to the roof space from any phone.

Total cost of the complete sensor network: approximately €50 to €100.

THE REMOVABLE WET WALL ACCESS PANEL:

The wet wall has a full-width removable access panel on the bathroom side. Four visible
screws. Remove four screws, lift the panel — the entire wet wall interior is accessible.
Every pipe, every joint, every connection visible. The salt reservoir visible at the
base. The waterproof membrane visible. A plumber inspects the complete wet wall in two
minutes. A buyer sees everything.

THE TWO-STAGE VIEWING:

Stage one — the aesthetic viewing. Buyer sees the finished house. Limewash walls,
walnut floor, bronze fittings, smart home, sedum roof. They respond to the result.
Standard house viewing.

Stage two — the technical inspection viewing for serious buyers. Remove the cap from
every inspection hatch in the house — fifteen minutes. The buyer probes every wall
cavity with a moisture meter. Removes the wet wall access panel — every pipe visible.
Reviews the complete sensor humidity log from day one — every wall cavity's condition
history on a phone screen. Reviews the photographic build record — every nail, every
layer, from foundation to ridge.

No conventional house offers this. A buyer who receives complete transparency into the
structure they are purchasing — down to the moisture history of every wall cavity — has
a level of confidence that no surveyor's report on a conventional house can provide.

The transparency is not just ethical. It is a selling feature. The house that shows you
everything it is made of commands a premium over the house that asks you to trust.

THE MEDIEVAL LOGIC AND THE MODERN TOOL:

This system sits at the intersection of two eras usually treated as opposites.

Medieval builders used what they had — salt, lime, pine tar, timber from the forest.
They applied these materials through direct physical logic: salt preserves because it
is hostile to biological life, lime kills mould because it is alkaline, pine tar
waterproofs because it is hydrophobic. No chemistry degree required. The logic is
permanent because physics is permanent.

Modern builders use what industry provides — synthetic paint, chemical pesticides,
engineered polymers, proprietary systems with proprietary failure modes.

This system takes the medieval logic and applies it with modern tools and modern
certified components. The nail gun drives the same nail the medieval carpenter drove,
faster and more consistently. The earth screw does what the stone foundation did,
reversibly. The OSB does what the hand-cut timber sheathing did, with factory-certified
consistency. The EPAL pallet provides certified structural timber that medieval builders
would have selected by hand from the forest.

Neither era alone produces this result. Medieval building with medieval tools was slow
and dependent on local skill. Modern building with modern systems is fast but complex
and expensive. The combination — medieval logic, modern tools, certified components —
is faster than medieval, cheaper than modern, and more robust than both.

Physical validation through prototype construction is the single most important next
step. The RCP testing protocol in Appendix H defines exactly how that validation will
be conducted and published. Every component in this system is independently certified
for loads that far exceed single storey domestic demands. The prototype will confirm
what overengineered components already predict.

A hundred years ago ordinary people built their own houses. Not professionals. Not
trained builders. People with no capital, no power tools, no engineered materials, and
no instruction manuals. They cut trees from the forest for rafters. They collected free
sawdust from the timber yard for insulation. They used limewash and pine tar because
that was what was available. No nail guns. No impact drivers. No OSB. No I-joists. No
earth screws. No structural adhesive.

Those houses are still standing. When you renovate a century-old rural house you can
see immediately that it was not built by professionals. The angles are not perfectly
square. The timber is rough-cut. The insulation is whatever was free. And yet the
structure survived a hundred years of weather, use, and neglect.

Today we have impact drivers, nail guns, structural adhesives, and engineered components
that those builders could not have imagined. We have OSB that is stronger and more
consistent than any hand-cut timber they worked with. We have I-joists that span
further and weigh less than the solid timber rafters they dragged from the forest. We
have earth screws that install in minutes where they dug foundations by hand. We have

APPENDIX E: FLOOR STRUCTURE — 2x6 SOLID TIMBER JOISTS
================================================================================

The floor structure in this system uses 38x140mm (2x6) solid C16 softwood joists. This is
simpler, cheaper, and more forgiving than engineered I-joists for the floor application.
No routing equipment required. No specialist fabrication. Widely understood by any structural
engineer. Available at every builders merchant in Portugal.

SPECIFICATION:
  Joist size: 38 x 140mm C16 softwood minimum
  Spacing: 400mm centres
  Bearing: directly on earth screws and treated sill plate
  Salt layer: 30mm coarse marine salt in the base of every floor joist bay
  Insulation: glass wool 140mm between joists above salt layer
  Deck: P5 T&G moisture resistant chipboard 22mm screwed to joist tops

SALT LAYER IN FLOOR BAYS:

Before laying the insulation between floor joists, pour a 30mm layer of coarse marine salt
across the base of every floor joist bay. The salt sits on the one-time pallets or membrane
below the joists and creates a permanent biological exclusion zone in the floor void.

Any moisture that enters the floor cavity — from ground vapour, a plumbing leak in the
wet wall, or any other source — contacts the salt immediately. The moisture becomes
saturated brine. Saturated brine is hostile to every organism that destroys timber:
bacteria, fungi, mould, termites, and rodents. A mouse that enters the floor void and
dies is desiccated rapidly by the salt before decomposition begins. A plumbing fault
that leaks slowly into the floor void saturates the salt and keeps the timber in a
brine-impregnated state — salt-impregnated wood is more resistant to rot than untreated
dry wood, not less. The leak is harmful without the salt. With the salt it becomes a
preservative condition.

The salt does not compress significantly under floor loads — a 30mm layer of coarse
salt is effectively rigid under the weight of glass wool insulation. It does not affect
the structural performance of the floor joists.

Cost: approximately 60 litres of salt per bay at 400mm joist centres across a 7m wide
house — approximately 17 bays — total approximately 1,000 litres = one cubic metre of
salt for the full floor. At €80 to €150 per cubic metre delivered: approximately
€80 to €150 total for the floor salt layer. Combined with the panel base salt, the
entire salt insurance system for the house costs under €250.

SPAN TABLE REFERENCE:
At 400mm centres, 38x140mm C16 softwood spans up to 3.0m under domestic floor loading
of 1.5 kN/m2 (EN 1991-1-1) with acceptable deflection. For spans greater than 3.0m,
increase to 38x184mm (2x8) or reduce centres to 300mm. Engineer to confirm for specific
project spans.

EARTH SCREW BEARING:
Joists bear directly on the earth screw head plates and on the perimeter sill plate.
No joist hangers required at intermediate earth screw positions — joists simply rest on
the bearing plate and are toe-screwed for lateral stability.

NOTE: For roof structure, always use CE-marked I-joists with manufacturer span tables.
The 2x6 floor joist system is for the floor only. Roof loads, wind uplift, and long-term
creep require certified performance data that only CE-marked I-joists provide.

APPENDIX H: RCP TESTING PROTOCOL
================================================================================

The RCP — Ribbed Composite Panel — is not yet certified. It is a new ribbed composite system
defined by its geometry and validated through testing rather than assumption. The following
protocol defines the four tests required to validate structural performance and produce
publishable safety margins.

Each test has two phases:
Phase A — Measured proof test: load to design value, record result
Phase B — Failure test: load to destruction, record failure mode and ultimate load

Results are presented in the format:
  Expected: X kg
  Tested: Y kg
  Safety margin: Y/X times

TEST 1 — WIND SIMULATION: RACKING RESISTANCE
Purpose: simulate wind pushing wall panel outward — lateral load resistance
Setup: bottom of panel fixed rigidly to ground frame. Winch pulls top plate outward.
Dynamometer inline to measure force. Laser or dial gauge measures top displacement.
Outputs: force versus displacement curve, stiffness, failure mode and location.

TEST 2 — AXIAL COMPRESSION: ROOF LOAD SIMULATION
Purpose: simulate vertical load from roof bearing on wall panel
Setup: panel standing upright. Hydraulic jack with spreader beam applies load to top plate.
Load applied incrementally in 10kN steps.
Outputs: load capacity, shortening under load, buckling behaviour, crushing failure mode.

TEST 3 — OUT-OF-PLANE BENDING: PANEL STIFFNESS
Purpose: measure panel stiffness as a spanning element — wind pressure on wall face
Setup: panel simply supported at top and bottom edges. Sandbags or water bags applied as
uniform distributed load across face. Laser measures midspan deflection.
Outputs: load versus deflection curve, effective stiffness, comparison with Eurocode 5
calculated value.

TEST 4 — FULL SYSTEM PULL-APART: COMPOSITE BOND STRENGTH
Purpose: measure ultimate composite strength — confirm wood failure before bond failure
Setup: one face of panel anchored to fixed frame. Tractor or winch pulls other face.
Dynamometer inline.
Outputs: maximum load at failure, failure mode — wood tear confirms bond stronger than
timber, clean bond peel indicates adhesive failure — weakest link identification.

MEASUREMENT EQUIPMENT:
Digital crane scale or load cell, 5 to 10 tonne capacity with peak hold function.
Laser distance meter or dial gauge for deflection measurement.

PUBLICATION FORMAT:
All results published in full including failures. The prototype does not need to pass every
test on the first attempt — it needs to produce interpretable data against the defined
baseline of this thesis. A failure is a result. A result is information. Information is
the point.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Hestia Foundation is the institutional vehicle for the RCP housing system
described in this thesis. It exists to research, document, certify, and disseminate
a low-cost self-build housing methodology that directly addresses three of the EU's
most urgent policy priorities: affordable housing supply, construction sector
innovation, and low-carbon building systems.

The EU currently has more money available for affordable housing innovation than at
any point in its history. The European Commission has committed €43 billion under
the 2021-2027 budget period for housing, with €10 billion additional in 2026-2027,
and €375 billion mobilised from partner financial institutions by 2029 (European
Commission, 2026). The first-ever EU heads of state summit on housing is scheduled
for 2026. The New European Bauhaus Facility alone has a yearly indicative budget of
€120 million for research and innovation in the built environment.

The Hestia Foundation is positioned at the exact intersection of every programme
the EU is currently funding.

THE CONTEXT THE EU HAS ALREADY CONFIRMED:

Since 2013, house prices across the EU have risen by over 60 percent. Only 6 to 7
percent of EU housing stock is social housing. Short-term rentals surged 93 percent
between 2018 and 2024. Europe needs to build more than 2 million homes per year —
approximately 650,000 more annually than the 1.6 million currently built. Meeting
this demand requires investment of approximately €153 billion per year. The
Commission's European Affordable Housing Plan explicitly calls for innovation in
the construction sector, simplified permitting, and scaling of innovative building
solutions (European Commission, 2026).

The RCP system addresses every single one of these goals simultaneously.
