01 - The Manifesto

Housing stopped being about homes.

In the 1970s, two years of average salary bought a home in Sweden. Today it takes eight. In Portugal, where we are building, it takes nineteen years of gross income to pay for an average property on an average salary. The house has not changed. The system has.

The financialization of shelter

Housing prices have detached from wages and attached to investment returns. Private equity funds, pension funds, and real estate investment trusts do not buy homes to live in. They buy them as yield-producing assets. A family wants a place to raise children. A fund wants 7% annual return. These are incompatible goals, and the fund has more capital.

When a house becomes an asset class, every efficiency in construction gets captured as margin, not passed on as affordability. Every innovation becomes intellectual property, not public good. The result is prices rise, quality stagnates, and the people who actually need shelter are priced out of the market they were supposed to serve.

Today a family in Portugal pays roughly €700 per month for thirty years to own a home. That is the equivalent of seventeen years of their entire income, handed to a bank, for the right to live under a roof. Across Europe, house prices have risen more than 60% since 2013. Europe needs to build more than two million homes per year and is failing.

This is not a shortage of materials. There is no shortage of wood, or land, or labour. It is a shortage of access. The home became a financial product. The family became the customer. And the knowledge of how to build, once common, became the property of an industry.

Six principles

I

Modern construction is broken by design.

A conventional house is an orchestra of complexity. An architect, a structural engineer, a project manager, a main contractor, and a dozen subcontracted trades, each playing a different instrument, none reading the same score. The conductor spends most of the budget managing the gap between what was drawn and what gets built. Every layer adds cost. Every handoff adds risk.

The complexity is not an accident. Complexity justifies the intermediation. A process that anyone could follow would not support the fees. So the process stays complex, the knowledge stays enclosed, and the price stays high.

We believe the opposite is possible. That a house can be a kit of standard parts. That the parts can be assembled by ordinary people in a defined sequence. That the quality can be higher, not lower, because the difficulty has been engineered out rather than papered over.

II

The materials already exist. All of them.

There are roughly 500 million EPAL Euro pallets in active circulation in Europe. They are made in every member state, from low-grade timber, to a single standardised specification: 1200 by 800 by 145 millimetres, certified, heat-treated, dimensionally exact. They are the most quietly standardised structural object on the continent.

We did not invent a material. We noticed one.

The Hestia system takes the pallet and uses it as the ribbed core of a structural composite panel, bonded between two sheets of oriented strand board. This is the same principle as the Structural Insulated Panel, a proven technology used in construction for fifty years. The commercial SIP uses a core of petroleum foam, which cannot be produced at civilisational scale and does not belong in a low-carbon future. We replace the foam with a timber skeleton that Europe already produces in effectively unlimited quantity.

The roof uses engineered I-joists, available from any builders merchant. The insulation is mineral wool, blown in by a contractor. The walls are clad in timber and finished with lime. Every single component is off the shelf, certified, and proven. There is no new material in this system. There is nothing to invent.

You do not need premium materials to build a premium home. You need the right materials in the right places, assembled in the right order.

III

Local assembly is the point, not a compromise.

The future of manufacturing is not one enormous factory. It is a distributed network of small local producers, making standardised components close to where they are used. This is more resilient, more flexible, and it keeps the value in the local economy.

A Hestia panel can be fabricated by four people with a nail gun and a glue applicator at a rate of five to six panels an hour. A team like that can produce the panel set for an entire family home in a single day. The work can happen in a barn, a workshop, a car park, or on the building site itself. It requires no computer-controlled machinery and no specialist trade. The skill required is attention, not apprenticeship.

This means the system scales the way an idea scales, not the way a factory scales. Anyone can pick it up. Anyone can teach it. The person who builds one home can help a neighbour build the next, and teach ten people to do the same. The knowledge compounds at the speed of a conversation.

IV

Open systems beat proprietary systems.

Bricks are open source. Nobody owns the brick. That is precisely why the brick is everywhere, why it has been improved for thousands of years, and why no single company can hold the world's builders hostage to its price.

Hestia is open source, like bricks.

Every drawing, every specification, every step of the protocol, every lesson learned, is published freely under a Creative Commons licence. Anyone can use it, adapt it, improve it, and build on it, for any purpose, including commercial, as long as they credit the source and keep it open.

We take our inspiration from WikiHouse, who proved that open-source building systems can generate real community momentum. From Open Source Ecology, who published fifty industrial machines openly and watched people build them everywhere. And from Linux, the operating system that runs most of the world, built by a distributed community giving their work away, which outcompeted every proprietary alternative precisely because it was open.

A proprietary housing system can be bought, enclosed, and shut down. An open one cannot. Once the knowledge is published and held by a community, it is permanent. It belongs to everyone. That is the only foundation on which affordable housing can actually be built at scale.

A proprietary system has a customer. An open system has a community.

V

A home should not require a debt sentence.

The Hestia system is designed so that a family can save, build, and own, without a mortgage, without a thirty-year commitment, without handing the majority of their working life to a lender for the right to live under a roof they built themselves.

A bank does not build a house. A family does. Yet the bank receives more years of a family's labour than the family receives years in their home. The system has inverted the relationship between builder and beneficiary. Hestia inverts it back.

A single parent on a median salary, with €15,000 saved and a €30,000 loan, can afford a three-bedroom home. The total materials cost for a complete 84 square metre family home stays under €50,000. It is our job to make that enough. It is our job to make the numbers work.

This is not charity. This is not subsidy. This is structural affordability, achieved by removing the intermediation that inflates cost, and by publishing the knowledge that enclosure has kept expensive.

VI

We publish what works, and what does not.

This is not an alternative housing idea. This is not a design concept. This is an engineering project, approached with the same discipline as any other system that people will live inside.

We document constraints openly. We publish failed iterations. We test every assumption. We measure thermal performance, structural deflection, moisture content, and energy balance, and we publish the data. The system improves in public, the way Linux improved in public, because scrutiny makes things better.

We have not built the prototype yet. Here is exactly what we will test, and the protocol we will follow. When it is built, every claim on this site will be backed by data and a receipt. We would rather show you precisely where every euro goes and let you check our arithmetic than ask you to believe a slogan.

The home is the oldest human institution. Hestia was the goddess of the hearth, the fire at the centre of the house around which the family gathered. A home is not a financial product. It is the place where a life is lived. We intend to make that place accessible again.

02 - The System

Five certified components. One complete home.

Every part is off-the-shelf. Nothing is custom. Nothing is scarce.

01

EPAL Euro Pallet

1200 × 800 × 145mm, ISPM 15, heat-treated

The ribbed structural core. Dimensionally exact. Certified. 500 million in circulation across Europe. Available from any logistics depot for €8-12.

02

OSB3 Board

2500 × 1250 × 15mm, EN 300, moisture resistant

The two structural skins bonded to the pallet core. The same material used in every SIP and timber frame wall already built. Any builders merchant.

03

Engineered I-Joist

STEICO SJ200 / SJ240 or equivalent

Roof and floor spanning members. Lighter than solid timber. Straighter. Longer spans. Used in 80%+ of new timber frame construction. Any timber merchant.

04

Helical Ground Screw

68mm diameter, 15-25 kN per screw

The foundation. No excavation. No concrete. No curing. Installed in three hours by two people with a handheld driver. Fully reversible. Any soil.

05

Structural PU Adhesive + Ring Shank Nails

EN 204 D4 class PU, 3.1 × 90mm nails

The bond that makes it composite. Continuous adhesive plus mechanical fastening creates true composite action. The OSB skins and timber core acting as one structural unit. Any builders merchant.

03 - Assembly

Under two weeks to weathertight.

Everything slow happens before assembly day. Assembly day is pure connection.

BEFORE

Prefabrication

Flexible, over weeks

Ground screws installed. Panels fabricated flat on a jig. Cladding pre-painted. Windows pre-fitted. Every panel finished and cured, stacked under cover, waiting.

→
DAY 0-1

Foundation + First Lift

3-4 hours

Sill beams on ground screws. Corner panels lifted and bolted. The shape of the house appears in an afternoon. No concrete. No curing. No weather delays.

→
DAY 1-2

Walls Complete

4-6 hours

Wall panels connected with M12 bolts at 400mm centres. Door and window openings framed. Shear diaphragm complete. The structure is rigid.

→
DAY 2-4

Roof + Weathertight

8-12 hours

I-joists drop into hangers. Steel roofing fixed. Guttering complete. The house is dry. Ready for insulation, electrics, and interior fit-out.

05 - Research & Data

We publish what works. And what does not.

Serious builders talk about constraints. Scammy projects talk only about vision.

Lateral bracing
→ Active

Corner panels and shear walls provide bracing under our assumptions. The OSB-faced panels create shear diaphragms. Full diaphragm capacity will be verified with racking tests at LNEC.

Condensation risk
→ Active

The assembly is vapour-open. OSB is vapour-permeable. Rock wool is vapour-permeable. Interstitial condensation risk will be quantified with 12-month monitoring and WUFI modelling.

Code approval
→ Active

Portuguese building code allows engineered timber systems with stamped calculations. An RCP panel is not a standard product with an ETA. We need project-specific calculations and test evidence.

Ground screw capacity
→ Active

Helical ground screws are proven in Northern European soils. Algarve soils are variable: sandy near coast, rocky inland, clay in valleys. On-site pull-out tests before installation.

Fire rating
→ Active

Target: REI 30. Raw timber plus OSB plus rock wool provides natural fire protection through char layer formation. Full-scale panel burn test scheduled for Q3 2026.

Long-term creep
? Open

Wood creeps under sustained load. The magnitude of long-term deformation in an RCP panel is unknown. 12-month monitoring includes LVDT deflection sensors on critical joints.
